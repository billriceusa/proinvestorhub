## What Is Cap Rate?

Cap rate (capitalization rate) measures what a property earns relative to what it costs, assuming you paid cash. A 7% cap rate on a $300,000 property means it generates $21,000 in net operating income per year.

Cap rate is the standard first screen for buy-and-hold investors because it strips out financing. Two investors buying the same property with different loans will see the same cap rate — which makes it an honest comparison tool across deals and markets.

What cap rate does not measure: appreciation, future rent growth, financing costs, or tax benefits. Think of it as the foundation of your analysis, not the whole building.

---

## The Cap Rate Formula

```
Cap Rate = Net Operating Income (NOI) ÷ Current Market Value × 100
```

Two inputs. That's it.

**Net Operating Income (NOI)** = Annual gross rental income – all operating expenses

Operating expenses include:
- Property taxes
- Insurance
- Property management fees (typically 8–12% of rent)
- Maintenance and repairs
- Vacancy allowance (5–10% is standard)
- Any utilities you cover
- HOA fees (if applicable)

**Mortgage payments are excluded.** Cap rate is always pre-debt. That's what makes it useful for comparison — it separates the property's income potential from your financing choices.

**Current Market Value** = What you're paying for it, or what it would sell for today.

## How to Calculate Cap Rate: Step-by-Step Example

Take a property renting for $2,200/month in Indianapolis:

| Line Item | Annual Amount |
|---|---|
| Gross rental income | $26,400 |
| Vacancy allowance (7%) | −$1,848 |
| Adjusted gross income | $24,552 |
| Property taxes | −$1,470 |
| Insurance | −$1,200 |
| Property management (9%) | −$2,210 |
| Maintenance reserve | −$1,500 |
| **Net Operating Income (NOI)** | **$18,172** |
| Purchase price | $175,000 |
| **Cap Rate** | **10.4%** |

That's well above market for Indianapolis (where the average is around 8.1%) — which might mean you're getting a great deal, or that the expenses are being underestimated. Always verify your numbers.

## What Is a Good Cap Rate for Rental Property?

This is the question every investor asks, and the honest answer is: it depends on your strategy and market.

Here's how experienced investors think about it:

### By Strategy

| Strategy | Target Cap Rate | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Pure cash flow (Midwest/South markets) | 7%–10%+ | Needs strong income to justify management overhead |
| BRRRR (buy, rehab, rent, refinance, repeat) | 8%–12% at purchase | Must be high enough before refinancing to still cash flow after refi |
| Cash flow + appreciation hybrid | 5%–7% | Accepting lower yield for markets with stronger rent growth |
| Appreciation play (gateway cities) | 3%–5% | Investors betting on long-term value, not current income |

### The Rule of Thumb That Matters Most

Your cap rate needs to beat your cost of capital. If you're financing with a DSCR loan at 7.5%, a property with a 6% cap rate is already cash-flow negative on an unlevered basis. You'd need exceptional rent growth to make that work.

Before committing to any deal, run your cap rate alongside your [cash-on-cash return](/calculators/cash-on-cash) — that's where financing enters the picture.

## Cap Rate vs. Cash-on-Cash Return vs. DSCR: Which Metric Do You Use When?

Investors regularly conflate these three metrics. They're related but measure different things.

| Metric | Formula | Includes Financing? | Best Used For |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Cap Rate** | NOI ÷ Market Value | No | Screening deals; comparing properties without financing distortion |
| **Cash-on-Cash Return** | Annual Cash Flow ÷ Total Cash Invested | Yes | Measuring your actual return on invested capital |
| **DSCR** | NOI ÷ Annual Debt Service | Yes | Qualifying for a DSCR loan; lender's view of deal viability |

**Use cap rate to screen.** It's the fastest way to filter out bad deals before you spend time on financing analysis.

**Use cash-on-cash to decide.** Once you have a loan scenario, [cash-on-cash return](/calculators/cash-on-cash) tells you what you're actually earning on your down payment each year.

**DSCR is your lender's number.** Most [DSCR lenders](/lenders) require a minimum ratio of 1.20–1.25. A property with a cap rate of 7% may easily clear that threshold; one at 4.5% in a high-rate environment often won't without a larger down payment.

A property with a 6% cap rate financed at 7.5% interest is underwater on DSCR before you even account for amortization. Run all three numbers before making an offer.

## How Cap Rate Fits the BRRRR Strategy

The [BRRRR method](/calculators/brrrr) (Buy, Rehab, Rent, Refinance, Repeat) creates a specific cap rate challenge: the cap rate you analyze at purchase isn't the same as the cap rate after renovation.

Here's why it matters:

**At purchase:** A distressed property might yield a 12–15% cap rate based on post-renovation rents, which is why BRRRR practitioners hunt deeply discounted properties.

**After refinancing:** The cash-out refinance pulls your equity back out, which means your effective cost basis changes. The cap rate doesn't change, but your cash-on-cash return might swing dramatically.

**The BRRRR cap rate rule of thumb:** You want post-rehab cap rate to sit at least 200–300 basis points above your refinance rate. If DSCR loans are at 7.5%, you want a stabilized cap rate of at least 9.5–10.5% to have comfortable cash flow after refinancing.

## 5 Common Cap Rate Mistakes (and How to Avoid Them)

**1. Using gross rent instead of NOI**  
The most common beginner error. Always subtract vacancy, management fees, taxes, insurance, and maintenance before dividing. Using gross rent overstates cap rate by 20–40%.

**2. Underestimating maintenance**  
A useful benchmark: budget 1–1.5% of property value per year for maintenance on a stabilized property. An older home or one coming out of renovation should be budgeted higher. [VERIFY: Confirm this aligns with current industry benchmarks in your specific market.]

**3. Ignoring vacancy**  
Use a vacancy factor even if the current tenant has lived there for five years. A standard 5% vacancy allowance is a minimum — use 8–10% in markets with higher turnover.

**4. Comparing cap rates across markets as if they're equivalent**  
A 7% cap rate in Memphis reflects a very different risk profile than 7% in Indianapolis. Always contextualize against local market benchmarks (see the table above).

**5. Using cap rate for short-term rentals**  
Cap rate assumes stable annual income. STR revenue is highly seasonal and dependent on occupancy rates. Use [STR-specific revenue modeling](/calculators) instead.

## When Cap Rate Is Not the Right Metric

Cap rate breaks down in several scenarios:

- **Fix-and-flip:** No stabilized rental income to calculate NOI — use ROI and ARV instead
- **Development deals:** No income history; use pro forma projections and different return metrics
- **Short-term rentals:** Income too variable for accurate NOI; occupancy-rate modeling is more appropriate
- **Properties you already own:** Use current market value (not original purchase price) for an accurate picture of where your investment stands today
- **Portfolios:** When analyzing multiple properties, aggregate NOI and total value, but don't assume individual property cap rates will average cleanly
