# HELOC on Rental Property: The Investor's Decision Framework (2026)

Yes — you can get a HELOC on a rental property. But most real estate investors discover the harder truth quickly: fewer than a handful of lenders nationwide actually offer them on non-owner-occupied properties, the requirements are stricter than on a primary home, and in many cases a DSCR cash-out refinance is the smarter move anyway. This guide cuts through the generic "can you do it?" content and answers the question that actually matters: **should you do it, and if so, how?**

---

## What Is a HELOC on a Rental Property?

A Home Equity Line of Credit (HELOC) is a revolving line of credit secured by a property you own. Unlike a lump-sum loan, you draw funds as needed during a draw period (typically 5–10 years), pay interest only on what you use, then repay the principal over a repayment period (typically 10–20 years).

On a rental property, the mechanics are the same — but the property securing the credit line is one you don't live in. That small distinction changes almost everything: the approval standards are tighter, the lender pool is dramatically smaller, and the rates are higher.

**The key terms:**
- **Draw period:** 5–10 years (interest-only payments)
- **Repayment period:** 10–20 years (principal + interest)
- **Maximum CLTV (Combined Loan-to-Value):** Usually 75–80% for investment properties vs. 85–90% for primary residences
- **Rate structure:** Variable, tied to prime rate

## HELOC on Rental Property Requirements (2026)

Qualifying for a HELOC on a rental property is meaningfully harder than on your primary home. Here's what lenders typically require:

### Equity Requirements

Most lenders want at least 20–25% equity in the investment property. In practice, that means your combined loan-to-value (CLTV) — your existing mortgage balance plus the HELOC limit, divided by the property's appraised value — must stay at or below 75–80%.

**Example:** A rental property appraised at $300,000 with a $180,000 mortgage outstanding.
- Equity: $120,000 (40%)
- Maximum HELOC at 80% CLTV: $300,000 × 0.80 – $180,000 = **$60,000**

Run your own numbers with the [Rental Cash Flow Calculator](/calculators/rental-cashflow) to see how a HELOC payment affects your monthly NOI.

### Credit Score Standards

Most lenders require a 700–720 minimum FICO for an investment property HELOC. Compare that to the 620–680 they'd accept on a primary residence. The premium exists because lenders know borrowers are statistically more likely to default on an investment property than their own home when finances tighten.

[VERIFY: Confirm current minimum FICO requirements have not shifted since Q1 2026 — some lenders have moved thresholds.]

### Debt-to-Income Ratio

DTI requirements vary, but most lenders use a 43–50% back-end DTI cap. Importantly, some — not all — will count your rental income to offset the property's mortgage payment when calculating DTI. If your lender won't credit rental income, expect a tougher qualification hurdle.

### Cash Reserves

Six months of PITI (principal, interest, taxes, insurance) reserves for each investment property you own is a common requirement. Some lenders extend that to all mortgaged properties in your portfolio. For investors with 5+ properties, this can mean significant idle cash.

## HELOC vs. DSCR Cash-Out Refinance: The Real Choice

Most real estate investors frame this as "how do I get a HELOC?" when the better question is "which equity extraction method fits my strategy?"

Here's how they compare:

| | HELOC on Rental | DSCR Cash-Out Refi |
|---|---|---|
| **Rate** | Variable (prime + 1–3%) | Fixed (currently ~7.5–9%) |
| **Access** | Revolving draw; pay as you go | Lump sum at close |
| **Term** | 5–10 yr draw + repayment | 30-year amortization |
| **Qualification** | Full DTI + income docs | Property income only (no W-2) |
| **Lender availability** | Very limited | Wide; [20+ lenders](/lenders/dscr-loans) |
| **LLC allowed** | Rarely | Yes, most DSCR lenders |
| **Keeps existing rate** | Yes | No — replaces first mortgage |
| **Prepayment penalty** | None typically | Yes, 3–5 year step-down common |

**When the HELOC wins:**
- Your existing first mortgage has a rate you don't want to disturb (pre-2022 sub-5% rate)
- You need a revolving credit line, not a lump sum (ongoing renovations, opportunistic buying)
- You have a high-equity property and need modest liquidity ($50K–$150K range)

**When DSCR cash-out refinance wins:**
- You want to close in an LLC or entity name
- You need a larger lump sum and want fixed-rate certainty
- You don't have a lender offering investment HELOCs in your state
- You want to consolidate and simplify to one monthly payment

For most investors actively scaling a portfolio, the DSCR cash-out refinance is more accessible, more lender-competitive, and eliminates the qualification friction of proving personal income. Browse the [lender directory](/lenders) to compare DSCR lenders side by side.

## How Real Estate Investors Actually Use a Rental Property HELOC

When an investment property HELOC is the right tool, here are the scenarios where it delivers real value:

### BRRRR Strategy: Funding the Rehab Phase

In the [BRRRR strategy](/start-here) (Buy, Rehab, Rent, Refinance, Repeat), the equity from a stabilized rental can fund the down payment or rehab costs on the next acquisition — without selling the first property or drawing cash reserves.

**Example:** You own a rental property free-and-clear valued at $280,000. A $150,000 HELOC at 80% CLTV gives you a revolving pool to fund acquisitions. You draw $80K for a fix-and-flip purchase and rehab, then repay after the refi. The line resets, ready for the next deal.

### Building a Portfolio War Chest

Rather than waiting to accumulate a down payment for the next property, a HELOC on an existing rental converts equity into deployable capital. You draw only what you need at deal close, avoiding carrying interest on idle funds.

### Renovation Without Disrupting Operations

If you're renovating a portfolio of rentals unit-by-unit, a revolving HELOC lets you draw, renovate, re-lease at higher rent, and repay — cycling the same credit line across multiple units without closing costs on each draw.

## Pros and Cons of a Rental Property HELOC

**Pros for real estate investors:**

- **Preserves your first mortgage rate** — if you locked a sub-6% DSCR loan in 2022–2023, a HELOC lets you tap equity without giving that rate up
- **Revolving access** — draw what you need, when you need it; interest accrues only on the outstanding balance
- **No prepayment penalties** — unlike most DSCR loans, HELOCs don't penalize early repayment
- **Interest may be tax-deductible** — when funds are used for rental property improvements or acquisitions, interest is typically deductible as a business expense on Schedule E [VERIFY: Confirm IRS treatment under current tax law — consult a CPA]
- **Doesn't affect LLC/entity structure** — though most HELOC lenders don't allow LLC borrowers, the property can still be in an LLC if you personally guarantee

**Cons:**

- **Variable rate risk** — a 250bps rate hike turns a 7.5% HELOC into a 10% HELOC fast; model worst-case with the [Cash-on-Cash Calculator](/calculators/cash-on-cash)
- **Tiny lender pool** — supply and demand works against you in rate negotiations
- **Income documentation required** — unlike DSCR loans, most HELOC lenders want your personal tax returns and W-2s; self-employed investors may face added friction
- **Foreclosure risk on the pledged property** — default puts the rental property at risk, not just your credit score
- **Harder to close in an entity** — most HELOC lenders require personal borrower; LLC-only investors are largely shut out

## Tax Implications

Interest on a HELOC secured by a rental property is generally deductible as a business expense when the proceeds are used for rental-related purposes — property improvements, acquiring additional rentals, or other investment property expenses. This treatment falls under Schedule E, not Schedule A, and doesn't run into the $750,000 home mortgage interest cap that applies to personal residences.

However, rules are nuanced. If you use HELOC proceeds for personal expenses (even temporarily), the deductibility of that portion can be challenged. Keep meticulous records of how every draw is deployed.

## How to Apply for a HELOC on a Rental Property

If you've decided a HELOC is the right move, here's how to approach it:

**Step 1: Calculate your available equity**
Use your last mortgage statement plus a conservative current market value estimate. Build in a 5–10% cushion below peak value. Use the [Cap Rate Calculator](/calculators/cap-rate) to verify the property's income-based value before relying solely on a comp-based estimate.

**Step 2: Pull your credit reports**
Check all three bureaus at AnnualCreditReport.com. Investment property HELOCs require 700+; anything below that needs attention before you apply.

**Step 3: Gather your documentation**
Unlike DSCR loans, investment property HELOC lenders typically require:
- Last 2 years of personal tax returns
- W-2s or 1099s (or P&L if self-employed)
- Current mortgage statement for the subject property
- Schedule E from your tax return showing rental income
- 6 months bank statements

**Step 4: Narrow your lender search**
Start with the short list above. Also check with local credit unions in your market — they often have the most favorable terms and are more willing to work with investors they know, even on non-standard products.

**Step 5: Get a competitive quote before committing**
Variable-rate products are particularly sensitive to lender margin differences. A 0.5% lower margin on the variable rate compounded over a 10-year draw period is meaningful. Use the [Lender Finder](/lenders/finder) to surface which lenders serve your specific loan type and state.

## Alternatives Worth Serious Consideration

If the HELOC path runs into walls (limited lenders, income documentation barriers, LLC mismatch), these alternatives are worth a structured comparison:

**DSCR Cash-Out Refinance**
The most popular equity extraction method for scaling investors. Qualifies on property income, no W-2s, closes in LLC. Currently 7.5–9.5% for 75% LTV cash-out. Best when you need a large lump sum and want payment certainty. See the [DSCR Loan page](/lenders/dscr-loans) for lender comparisons.

**Home Equity Loan on Rental Property**
Lump-sum cousin to the HELOC. Fixed rate, one-time draw. Same limited lender universe as the HELOC. Better for investors who want payment predictability and a defined payoff date.

**Cash-Out Conventional Refinance (if under 10 properties)**
If you're early in your investing journey (under 10 financed properties), conventional refinances offer the lowest rates (6.0–7.5%) with 75% LTV cash-out. Personal income qualification required. Rate advantage can be significant.

**Portfolio Line of Credit**
Some private lenders and smaller banks offer lines of credit collateralized by a portfolio of properties rather than a single property. Higher minimums ($500K+ in property value), but can be more flexible and offer LLC borrowing.

## The Bottom Line

A HELOC on a rental property is a real tool — but a specialized one. The lender pool is small, the requirements are tighter than a primary-home HELOC, and self-employed investors or LLC-only borrowers face structural barriers. Before you spend weeks chasing a product that may not be available in your state, compare it honestly against a DSCR cash-out refinance.

If your existing mortgage rate is worth protecting, or you need revolving access rather than a lump sum, the HELOC case is strong. If you're scaling fast, need entity-level borrowing, or want the widest lender competition, DSCR cash-out is usually the better play.
