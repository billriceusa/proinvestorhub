# HELOC on Rental Property: The Investor's Decision Guide (2026)

Yes — you can get a HELOC on a rental property, but fewer lenders offer them, the requirements are stricter, and the right move depends on your strategy. This guide covers exactly how a rental property HELOC works, what it costs compared to alternatives like a cash-out refinance or [DSCR loan](/lenders/dscr-loans), and the scenarios where it makes sense (and where it doesn't). If you've built equity in a rental and want to put it to work, here's the investor's framework.

---

## The Distinction Most Searchers Miss: Two HELOC Strategies

Before going further, there's an important split that competing guides gloss over. When investors search "HELOC on rental property," they typically mean one of two different things:

**Strategy A — HELOC on the rental itself:** You borrow against the equity in a property you already own as a rental. The rental is the collateral. This is the harder product to find and the subject of most of this guide.

**Strategy B — HELOC on your primary home to buy a rental:** You tap equity in your primary residence and use those funds as a down payment or purchase price on an investment property. This is more widely available and generally easier to qualify for — but it puts your home on the line.

The two strategies have completely different risk profiles, qualification requirements, and tax implications. Most bank-authored guides conflate them. This guide focuses on Strategy A — pulling equity out of a rental you already own — because that's the higher-leverage move for investors with an existing portfolio.

## What Lenders Actually Want: Requirements for a Rental Property HELOC

Rental property HELOCs are considered higher-risk loans than primary home HELOCs. If you can't make payments, you're more willing to walk away from a rental than from the house you live in. Lenders price that risk into their requirements:

| Requirement | Rental Property HELOC | Primary Residence HELOC |
|---|---|---|
| Minimum credit score | 700–720 | 650–680 |
| Maximum LTV | 75–80% combined | 85–90% |
| Minimum equity stake | 20% | 15% |
| DTI maximum | ~50% (rental income counted) | 43% |
| Cash reserves required | 6 months' payments | Often none |
| Appraisal type | In-person (sometimes multiple) | Desktop or in-person |
| Rate premium | 0.5–1.5% above primary rates | Baseline rate |

A few nuances worth noting:

**Your combined LTV matters more than equity alone.** If your rental is worth $350,000 and you still owe $120,000, your equity is $230,000 — but you won't be able to access all of it. Lenders cap combined loan-to-value (CLTV) at 75–80%, meaning your existing mortgage plus the new HELOC cannot exceed that threshold. In this example, the maximum CLTV of 80% on a $350K property equals $280,000. Subtract the $120,000 first mortgage and you can potentially draw up to $160,000 — assuming you meet all other requirements.

**Rental income counts toward your DTI.** Unlike some loan products that ignore or haircut rental income, most HELOC lenders will count your actual rental income (documented by lease agreements and two years of Schedule E) toward your qualifying income. This makes it easier to qualify if you're self-employed or have a thin W-2 relative to your investment income.

**Finding a lender is the hardest part.** Most major banks do not offer HELOCs on investment or non-owner-occupied properties. You'll typically need to look at specialty lenders, regional banks, credit unions, or online lenders with an investor product line. ## Working the Numbers: A Real Example

Here's a concrete scenario showing what a rental property HELOC can actually provide.

**The property:**
- Current market value: $350,000
- Remaining first mortgage balance: $140,000
- Your equity: $210,000 (60% equity stake)

**The HELOC calculation:**
- Maximum CLTV at 80%: $350,000 × 0.80 = $280,000
- Minus existing mortgage: $280,000 − $140,000 = **$140,000 maximum HELOC line**

**The cost:**
- Assume a HELOC rate of prime + 1.5% (roughly 9.0–9.5% in mid-2026 rate environment)
- Draw period: typically 10 years (interest-only payments)
- Monthly interest on a $140,000 draw: ~$1,050–$1,108/month
- Repayment period: 10–20 years on the principal after the draw period ends

**The deployment:**
You use the $140,000 line to fund a 25% down payment on a second rental at $560,000, keeping your existing rental cash flow intact while adding a second income-producing property to your portfolio. [VERIFY: check current average investment property down payment requirements in your target market — conventions vary by lender.]

The key question isn't whether you can access the equity — it's whether the return on the deployed capital exceeds the HELOC's cost of carry. If the new rental generates $1,800/month in [net operating income](/glossary/noi), and the HELOC costs $1,050/month in interest, you're cash-flow positive from day one while still holding equity appreciation in the original property.

## HELOC vs. Cash-Out Refi vs. DSCR Loan: The Investor's Decision Matrix

Rental property HELOCs compete directly with two other equity-access tools. The right choice depends on your rate environment, timeline, and how you plan to use the funds.

| Factor | Rental Property HELOC | Cash-Out Refinance | DSCR Cash-Out Refi |
|---|---|---|---|
| Rate type | Variable (prime-based) | Fixed | Fixed or ARM |
| Rate range (mid-2026) | ~8.5–10.5% | ~7.0–8.5% | ~7.5–9.0% |
| Access method | Draw as needed (revolving) | Lump sum | Lump sum |
| Affects existing mortgage? | No | Yes — replaces it | Yes — replaces it |
| Income verification | Full doc or limited | Full doc | Property cash flow only |
| Best for... | Flexible/ongoing needs | Rate improvement + cash out | Self-employed investors |
| Closes in... | 30–45 days | 30–60 days | 21–45 days |
| Risk if rates rise | Higher (variable rate) | None (fixed) | Depends on product |

**Use a HELOC when:**
- You need flexible draw access (renovations, deal-by-deal deployment)
- Your existing mortgage has a great rate you don't want to disturb
- You want the option to repay and re-draw over time
- You're planning multiple smaller investments rather than one large one

**Use a cash-out refinance when:**
- Current rates are lower than your existing mortgage rate (rare in 2026)
- You need a large lump sum and want fixed, predictable payments
- You're certain about the exact amount you need

**Use a DSCR cash-out refi when:**
- You're self-employed or your W-2 income doesn't support full-doc underwriting
- The property's [cash flow](/glossary/cash-flow) can carry the new debt service on its own
- You're scaling past the 10-property conventional loan limit

## How Rental Property HELOCs Power the BRRRR Strategy

If you're using the BRRRR method (Buy, Rehab, Rent, Refinance, Repeat), a rental property HELOC can serve as a powerful bridge between the "Refinance" and "Repeat" steps. Here's how it works in practice:

1. **Buy and Rehab**: You purchase a distressed property using short-term capital (hard money, private money, or personal funds).
2. **Rent**: You lease the property, establishing a rental income history.
3. **Refinance**: After 6–12 months of seasoning, you do a long-term refinance (typically a DSCR loan) to pull out your equity and repay short-term debt.
4. **HELOC as a revolving weapon**: Rather than waiting for each property to season before the next deal, you open a HELOC against a fully stabilized rental in your portfolio. This gives you a ready line of capital you can draw and repay in sync with each deal cycle without doing a full refinance each time.

The [BRRRR Calculator](/calculators/brrrr) can help you model exactly how much equity you'll build through the rehab and rent cycle, and whether it supports a HELOC draw once the property is seasoned.

For investors running multiple BRRRR cycles simultaneously, a rental property HELOC on a free-and-clear (or low-LTV) property acts as a deal engine: you draw to fund the next acquisition, complete the cycle, pay down the HELOC, and start again. This is a more capital-efficient approach than repeatedly refinancing the same property or taking on new hard money positions at 12–14%.

## Tax Treatment: What Investors Actually Need to Know

This is where a rental property HELOC diverges most sharply from a primary home HELOC, and most guides get it wrong.

**The general rule:** HELOC interest on a primary residence is only deductible if the funds are used to "buy, build, or substantially improve" the home that secures the loan. Use the funds for anything else — including buying a rental — and you lose the deduction.

**The investor exception:** When the HELOC is secured by a rental property and the funds are used for rental-related purposes, the interest is typically deductible as a business expense on Schedule E, not as home mortgage interest on Schedule A. This is generally more favorable, especially for investors who no longer itemize deductions.

Specifically:
- **HELOC on Rental A → funds improvements to Rental A**: Deductible on Schedule E as a rental expense.
- **HELOC on Rental A → used to buy Rental B**: Generally deductible as an investment interest expense — but you need to trace the funds carefully and consult a CPA about the applicable interest tracing rules.
- **HELOC on primary home → used to buy a rental**: The interest likely falls under investment interest expense rules, not home mortgage interest deduction. The deductibility depends on whether you have net investment income to offset.

[VERIFY: Tax treatment varies based on the use of funds, property structure (individual vs. LLC), and IRS guidance as of your tax year. Consult a CPA with real estate experience before making financing decisions based on expected deductibility.]

## When NOT to Use a HELOC on Your Rental Property

A rental property HELOC is a leverage tool, and leverage cuts both ways. Skip it when:

**You're planning to sell the property soon.** Opening a HELOC and then listing the property creates complications at closing. Some lenders require the HELOC to be paid off and closed before the sale can proceed.

**The rental is already cash flow negative.** Adding HELOC interest payments to a struggling property creates compounding stress. Fix the underlying cash flow problem first.

**You don't have a clear deployment plan for the funds.** A variable-rate line of credit with no deployed return is pure cost. Know exactly where the capital is going and model the return before you draw.

**Property values in your market are declining.** A HELOC against a property that subsequently drops in value can put you underwater — owing more on the combined first mortgage and HELOC than the property is worth. This complicates future refinancing or sale.

**Your reserves are already thin.** Lenders require 6 months of reserves, but that's a minimum, not a target. Running a rental portfolio with minimum reserves plus an open HELOC is a brittle position in a market with elevated vacancy or maintenance surprises.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Can I get a HELOC on a rental property if it's held in an LLC?**
Some lenders allow it, but many do not — it depends on the lender's underwriting guidelines and state laws regarding LLC-held real estate. LLC-held properties often require a personal guarantee and sometimes a lender-approved trust or operating agreement review. [VERIFY: Confirm current LLC policy with each lender before applying, as this varies significantly.]

**How long does it take to get a HELOC on a rental property?**
Typical timelines run 30–45 days for traditional lenders, though some online lenders have compressed this to 7–14 days with digital appraisals and automated underwriting. [VERIFY: Timeline varies by lender and market — confirm current expected timelines before planning a deal around HELOC funds.]

**Does a rental property HELOC affect my ability to get another mortgage?**
Yes. The HELOC line — even if undrawn — counts toward your total debt obligations and may affect your [LTV](/glossary/ltv) ratios on the underlying property. Drawn balances count directly against your DTI. Factor this into your financing strategy before opening a HELOC if you plan to take on more traditional mortgage financing.

**What's the difference between a HELOC and a home equity loan on a rental property?**
A HELOC is a revolving line you can draw and repay over time (typically 10-year draw period). A home equity loan is a lump sum with fixed payments from day one. For investment flexibility, HELOCs are usually preferable. For predictability (e.g., a specific renovation budget), home equity loans have cleaner payment structures.
