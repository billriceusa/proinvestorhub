## The Cash Flow Formula (And Why Most Investors Get It Wrong)

Rental property cash flow has one formula:

**Cash Flow = Gross Rental Income − Vacancy Loss − Operating Expenses − Mortgage Payment**

Simple enough. The problem is what people leave out.

Most first-time investors subtract mortgage and taxes. Experienced investors subtract *everything* — and the gap between those two calculations is often the difference between a deal that cash flows and one that quietly drains the bank account for three years.

Here is every line item that belongs in a real cash flow analysis:

### Gross Rental Income
Start with market rent, not asking rent. If the property is occupied at below-market rates, use what comparable units actually rent for in the current market. You can verify this at Zillow, Rentometer, or by calling two local property managers.

### Vacancy (5–8% of gross rent)
Even great rentals sit vacant between tenants. Budget 5% for stable markets (roughly 18 days per year). Use 8–10% in softer markets, seasonal markets, or anywhere with higher tenant turnover. This is the line item most beginners skip because "I already have a tenant." Price the property as if the tenant will eventually leave.

### Property Taxes
Get the actual assessed amount from the county, not the current owner's tax bill. In states where property taxes are reassessed at sale — California, Michigan, Florida — your tax bill can jump significantly at closing. [VERIFY: confirm which states trigger full reassessment at sale vs. use purchase price as assessed value]

### Insurance (Landlord Policy, Not Homeowner's)
A DP-3 landlord policy costs more than a homeowner's policy. Expect $800–$2,000 per year for a single-family rental depending on location, age, and coverage level. In coastal or disaster-prone states (Florida, Louisiana, parts of Texas), rates have risen 20–40% since 2023 as carriers have exited those markets. Get an actual quote before you close, not an estimate.

### Maintenance (5–10% of Gross Rent)
Budget 5% for newer or recently renovated properties. Budget 8–10% for older homes or anything with aging mechanicals (roof, HVAC, water heater). This reserve covers the refrigerator that dies in month seven, the fence section that falls in a storm, and the furnace replacement you didn't budget for. It is not optional — it is a cost of ownership.

### Property Management (8–12% of Collected Rent)
Include this even if you self-manage. Your time has value. If you plan to scale, you will eventually hire management — and a deal that only cash flows because you work for free is not a deal, it's a part-time job without pay. Most markets: 8–10% for single-family, 6–8% for multi-unit.

### CapEx Reserve (5–10% of Gross Rent)
Capital expenditures are big-ticket items: roof replacement, HVAC system, windows, plumbing overhaul. These are not monthly expenses, but they happen — and they are expensive. Set aside 5% for newer properties, up to 10% for anything over 20 years old. This reserve does not appear on your P&L each month, but it absolutely affects whether you make money over a 5–10 year hold.

### Mortgage Payment (Principal + Interest)
At current rates (investment properties were pricing at 7.0–7.75% on 30-year conventional loans in early 2026 [VERIFY: confirm current investment property rate ranges]), financing costs more than it did in 2020–2021. Run your actual mortgage payment — not a ballpark. Use our [Mortgage Calculator](/calculators/mortgage) or your lender's loan estimate.

## Real Deal: $280,000 Single-Family Rental in Cleveland, 2026

Let me run a real example — the kind of analysis I do before placing any offer.

**Property:** 3-bed/2-bath single-family rental, Cleveland suburb (Class B neighborhood)  
**Purchase price:** $280,000  
**Down payment:** 25% ($70,000)  
**Loan amount:** $210,000 at 7.25% / 30-year term  

| Line Item | Monthly | Annual |
|---|---|---|
| Gross Rent (market rent) | $1,900 | $22,800 |
| Vacancy (6%) | −$114 | −$1,368 |
| **Effective Rental Income** | **$1,786** | **$21,432** |
| Property Taxes | −$292 | −$3,500 |
| Insurance (landlord policy) | −$125 | −$1,500 |
| Maintenance (8%) | −$143 | −$1,716 |
| Property Management (10%) | −$179 | −$2,143 |
| CapEx Reserve (7%) | −$125 | −$1,500 |
| **Total Operating Expenses** | **−$864** | **−$10,359** |
| **Net Operating Income (NOI)** | **$922** | **$11,073** |
| Mortgage Payment (P+I) | −$1,432 | −$17,184 |
| **Net Cash Flow** | **−$510** | **−$6,111** |

This deal is cash flow negative. That is not automatically a dealbreaker — but it needs to be a deliberate choice, not a surprise.

Now let me adjust one assumption: the investor puts 30% down ($84,000) instead of 25%, reducing the loan to $196,000.

| Mortgage Payment (30% down) | −$1,338 | −$16,056 |
| **Net Cash Flow (30% down)** | **−$416** | **−$4,992** |

Still negative. To break even or generate cash flow at current rates on this property, the investor would need to either:
- Find the property at $240,000–$250,000 (roughly 10–15% below market)
- Raise the rent to $2,100+ (possible with light value-add rehab)
- Accept negative cash flow as an appreciation play in a market where prices are likely to grow

The calculator shows you the number. The strategy is what you do with it.

## What Is "Good" Cash Flow? Benchmarks by Property Type

There is no universal threshold. What "good" means depends on your strategy, the market, and your financing structure. Here are the benchmarks serious investors use:

### Single-Family Rentals
- **$100–$200/month per property:** Acceptable minimum for most investors. Provides some buffer and lets the asset appreciate while generating income.
- **$200–$400/month:** Strong. Most cash-flow-focused investors target this range.
- **$400+/month:** Excellent. Usually requires either a significant below-market purchase or a strong rent-to-price ratio market.

### Multi-Unit (Duplex, Triplex, Quad)
- **$100/unit/month:** Common floor. A duplex should clear at least $200/month combined.
- **$150–$300/unit/month:** Good. The fixed expense sharing (one roof, one furnace) makes multi-units more efficient per dollar invested.

### By Market Type
- **Class A markets (Austin, Denver, Phoenix):** Cash flow is compressed. Investors accept thin margins or slight negative cash flow for appreciation. Gross rent multipliers run high.
- **Class B markets (Cleveland, Kansas City, Memphis, Birmingham):** The sweet spot for cash flow. Rent-to-price ratios are more favorable, though insurance and property management quality vary.
- **Class C markets (Rust Belt cities, rural areas):** Higher gross yields but higher vacancy, maintenance, and management complexity. Run conservative numbers.

## The DSCR Connection: Does Your Deal Qualify for Investor Financing?

Here is the piece most cash flow articles miss: your cash flow analysis also tells you whether the property qualifies for a [DSCR loan](/blog/dscr-loans-explained-complete-guide-real-estate-investors-2026).

DSCR (Debt Service Coverage Ratio) is the metric lenders use to qualify investment property loans without requiring W-2 income verification. The formula:

**DSCR = Monthly Gross Rent ÷ Monthly Mortgage Payment (PITI)**

PITI = Principal + Interest + Taxes + Insurance

Most DSCR lenders require a minimum DSCR of 1.0–1.25. A DSCR of 1.0 means rent exactly covers the mortgage. A DSCR of 1.25 means rent covers 125% of the mortgage — the property has cushion.

Back to our Cleveland example:
- Gross rent: $1,900
- PITI: $1,432 (P+I) + $292 (taxes) + $125 (insurance) = $1,849
- DSCR: $1,900 ÷ $1,849 = **1.03**

This deal barely qualifies at 1.0 DSCR. Many lenders will fund it; some require 1.20 and won't touch it. Shopping lenders matters — and our [Lender Finder](/lenders/finder) can match you with DSCR lenders by state and loan type.

If your cash flow analysis is negative but your DSCR is above 1.0, you may still be able to finance the deal — the lender qualifies on rent vs. mortgage, not on your all-in investor cash flow.

## Cash Flow vs. Appreciation: A Strategy Question, Not a Math Question

Not every real estate investor needs or wants strong cash flow. Here is how to think about the tradeoff:

**Cash flow-first investors** want properties that generate monthly income, value stability over appreciation potential, and often focus on Midwest/Southeast markets. They use cash flow to fund their lifestyle or reinvest in more properties without needing additional capital.

**Appreciation-first investors** accept thin or negative monthly cash flow in markets where price growth is predictable: gateway cities, supply-constrained coastal markets, and high-demand Sunbelt metros. They cover negative cash flow from income or other assets and benefit at sale.

**The BRRRR investor** optimizes for equity at purchase, forces appreciation through renovation, then refinances to pull cash out — and expects cash flow after the refi to cover the new loan. Run these deals through our [BRRRR Calculator](/calculators/brrrr) because the cash flow analysis happens *after* the refinance, not at purchase price.

The mistake is not choosing the wrong strategy — it is choosing appreciation when you needed cash flow, or the reverse, because you did not think it through before buying.

## The 50% Rule: A 60-Second Pre-Calculator Screen

Before you open the calculator on every property, use the 50% Rule to screen deals quickly.

**50% Rule:** Operating expenses (excluding mortgage) will consume approximately 50% of gross rent over time. Use the remaining 50% to cover debt service. If there is nothing left after debt service, do not bother running full numbers — the deal is probably negative.

**Quick check:**
1. Take monthly gross rent
2. Multiply by 0.50 (your estimated operating expenses)
3. Subtract the estimated mortgage payment
4. If the result is positive, run full numbers. If it is zero or negative, look for a lower price or move on.

Example: $1,900 rent × 0.50 = $950. Mortgage = $1,432. $950 − $1,432 = −$482.

The 50% Rule confirmed what the full analysis showed — without running 15 line items. Use it to filter, not to decide.

## 5 Cash Flow Calculation Mistakes That Cost Investors Money

**1. Using asking rent instead of market rent.**  
Sellers often advertise above-market rent. Pull three comparable rentals from Zillow or call a local property manager before trusting the listing.

**2. Ignoring vacancy because the property is occupied.**  
Current tenants leave. Budget for it regardless of current occupancy status.

**3. Forgetting CapEx because "the property looks fine."**  
Major systems have replacement schedules. A 15-year-old roof is 15 years into a 20-year life. Budget for it.

**4. Excluding property management because "I'll self-manage."**  
Include it anyway. It reveals the real economics of the deal and protects you when life gets busy or you hire help.

**5. Using today's numbers without stress-testing.**  
Run the analysis at 8% vacancy instead of 5%. Run it with insurance $200/year higher. Run it with rent flat for 24 months. If the deal turns into a significant monthly loss under moderate stress, the margin is too thin.
