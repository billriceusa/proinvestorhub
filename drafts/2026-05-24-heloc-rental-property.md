# HELOC on a Rental Property: The Investor's Complete Guide (2026)

Yes, you can get a HELOC on a rental property — but most banks won't do it, the qualification bar is higher than on your primary home, and finding the right lender takes work. This guide is written for real estate investors who already own a rental and want to tap that equity to grow their portfolio, cover renovations, or bridge a gap between deals. We'll cover where to look, how to qualify, and how to decide whether a HELOC is actually the right tool versus a cash-out refinance or DSCR loan.

---

## Can You Get a HELOC on a Rental Property?

Yes — but with a critical asterisk. A HELOC on a rental property (also called an investment property HELOC or non-owner-occupied HELOC) is absolutely a real product. The problem is availability: most major banks, credit unions, and retail mortgage lenders have quietly stopped offering them. Walk into Chase or Bank of America and you'll likely be told they only do HELOCs on primary residences.

That's why investors end up on Reddit asking "has anyone done this?" The answer is yes — but you need to know where to look, and you need to understand that the terms are meaningfully different from a primary home HELOC.

**The short version of what makes this harder:**

- Minimum credit scores 700–720 (vs. 620–660 for primary residence)
- Max LTV of 75–80% (vs. 85–90% for primary)
- 6 months of cash reserves typically required
- Full income documentation, often including rent rolls and leases
- In-person appraisal (no drive-by or desktop appraisals)

If you meet those requirements, the product exists and can be a powerful tool.

---

## Why Most Banks Won't Do It

From a lender's risk perspective, a HELOC on a rental property carries more exposure than one on the home you live in. The reasoning:

If you run into financial trouble, you'll pay your primary mortgage first and let the rental go. The rental HELOC is a junior lien behind the first mortgage, which means in a foreclosure scenario the HELOC lender is last in line. If the property value has dropped or equity is thin, they might get nothing.

This risk calculation is why the major banks — who securitize loans and follow Fannie/Freddie guidelines — have mostly exited this product. The lenders that do offer investment property HELOCs tend to be:

- Specialty mortgage lenders (Better Mortgage is one notable exception)
- Regional and community banks that hold loans on their balance sheet
- Some credit unions with investment property programs
- Private lenders (higher cost, shorter terms, but more flexible)

## HELOC vs. Cash-Out Refi vs. DSCR Cash-Out: Which Is Right?

Before you pursue a HELOC, it's worth stress-testing whether a different product is actually a better fit for your situation. Here's how the three main equity-access options compare for rental property owners:

| | Investment Property HELOC | Cash-Out Refinance | DSCR Cash-Out Refi |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Rate structure** | Variable (prime + spread) | Fixed | Fixed |
| **Access method** | Revolving credit line | Lump sum at closing | Lump sum at closing |
| **Max LTV** | 75–80% | 70–75% | 75–80% |
| **Min credit score** | 700–720 | 680–700 | 620–680 |
| **Income verification** | Full doc + rent roll | Full doc (W-2/tax returns) | None — qualifies on rent |
| **Rate premium vs. primary** | +0.5–1.5% | +0.5–1.5% | +1–2% vs. conventional |
| **Best for** | Ongoing/uncertain capital needs | Lump sum at best available rate | Self-employed investors, no W-2 |
| **Disrupts existing mortgage** | No | Yes — resets rate and term | Yes |

**Choose a HELOC when:**
- You need capital on a rolling basis (e.g., ongoing renovations, deal flow)
- You have a great rate on your current mortgage you don't want to lose
- You want flexibility to draw down only what you need

**Choose a cash-out refinance when:**
- You need a large, single lump sum
- You can lower your rate at the same time
- Your current mortgage rate is already high

**Choose a DSCR cash-out when:**
- You're self-employed or your personal income doesn't reflect your actual finances
- You have 10+ financed properties (conventional limits don't apply to DSCR)
- You want to close in an LLC

## What It Takes to Qualify for an Investment Property HELOC

Here are the typical qualification benchmarks. These vary by lender, but these numbers represent what most investment property HELOC programs require in 2026:

### Credit Score
Most lenders require **700–720 minimum** for a rental property HELOC. Some specialty lenders will go as low as 680 with compensating factors (strong rental income, significant reserves). This is meaningfully higher than primary residence HELOCs, where 620–650 is common.

If your score is in the 680–700 range, improving it before applying could widen your lender options significantly.

### Equity / Loan-to-Value
You'll typically need at least **20–25% equity** in the property. Lenders measure this using combined LTV (CLTV), which adds your existing mortgage balance to the HELOC line and divides by the property value.

Example: A property appraised at $350,000 with a $200,000 mortgage has $150,000 in equity. At 80% CLTV, total debt allowed = $280,000. Subtract the $200,000 mortgage, and you have a maximum HELOC line of $80,000.

### Debt-to-Income (DTI)
This is where rental income matters. Most investment property HELOC lenders allow lenders to use **75% of gross rental income** to offset the property's expenses. If your rental income is $2,500/month, lenders typically credit you $1,875/month. Your DTI, combining all debt payments against all qualifying income (job income + rental offset), usually needs to be below 43–50%.

### Cash Reserves
Expect to demonstrate **6 months of PITI** (principal, interest, taxes, insurance) for both your primary residence and the rental property. On a $200,000 mortgage at 7% plus $600/month taxes/insurance, that's roughly $30,000+ in reserves. This is often the biggest stumbling block for investors.

### Property Condition and Appraisal
Plan for a full, in-person appraisal. Lenders want to verify condition and confirm value — no desktop appraisals. If the property has deferred maintenance, address it before applying. The appraiser will note any significant issues that could affect value or habitability.

### Documentation
You'll typically need:
- 2 years of tax returns (personal and any entity returns)
- Current leases for all units
- 2–3 months of bank and investment account statements
- Mortgage statements for all financed properties
- Appraisal (ordered by the lender)

## How Real Investors Use a Rental Property HELOC

The strongest use cases for a rental HELOC fit into three categories. Outside these, you should carefully evaluate whether the cost of the product (higher rate, fees, variable exposure) is justified.

### 1. Fund the Down Payment on the Next Property

This is the most common strategic use. You've built $80,000 in equity in rental #1. Instead of waiting to save the cash (or liquidating another investment), you tap a HELOC and use that capital as the down payment on rental #2.

The math only works if:
- Property #2 generates positive cash flow after all debt service (including the HELOC payment)
- You can service both the HELOC interest and rental #2's mortgage from rental income
- You have enough reserves to cover both properties during vacancy

Use our [Rental Cash Flow Calculator](/calculators/rental-cashflow) to stress-test this before you draw a dollar.

### 2. Fund a Rehab on the Same Property

You bought a rental below market because it needed work. Now you want to renovate to raise rents or refinance at a higher value. A HELOC lets you fund the renovation without disrupting your existing financing.

This is different from a fix-and-flip scenario (where a hard money loan is usually more appropriate). This is value-add on a property you intend to hold.

**Key consideration:** After the renovation, run a new cap rate analysis using the expected post-improvement rent. If the improved NOI supports a refinance at better terms, the HELOC may be a bridge to a better permanent financing structure. See how this works in the [BRRRR Calculator](/calculators/brrrr).

### 3. Emergency Reserve / Opportunity Fund

Some investors establish a HELOC and leave it undrawn — essentially creating a line of credit they can deploy quickly when a deal comes along. Unlike a cash-out refinance (where you pay interest from day one), a HELOC accrues interest only on what you draw.

This works well if you're in a market where deals move fast and you need to be able to move cash quickly without going through a full loan process each time.

**Risk to watch:** If property values drop and the lender re-appraises, they can freeze or reduce the line. This happened to many investors during the 2008 crisis. Don't count on a HELOC line as your only liquidity.

## Step-by-Step: How to Apply for a Rental Property HELOC

**Step 1: Know your equity position**
Get a rough market value estimate (Zillow, Redfin, comp search), subtract your existing mortgage balance, and calculate your CLTV at 80%. That's your theoretical maximum HELOC line.

**Step 2: Pull your credit reports**
Check all three bureaus at AnnualCreditReport.com. Dispute any errors. If your score is below 700, spend 3–6 months improving it before applying — the rate difference between 690 and 720 can be meaningful.

**Step 3: Find lenders who actually do this**
This is not the step to call your local bank and hope. Use [ProInvestorHub's Lender Finder](/lenders/finder) to identify lenders in your state with investment property HELOC programs. You're looking specifically for lenders with non-owner-occupied HELOC products — not just standard home equity programs.

**Step 4: Get 2–3 quotes**
Get Loan Estimates from at least two lenders. Compare:
- Intro rate vs. fully indexed rate (prime + margin)
- Draw period length (5 vs. 10 years)
- Repayment period
- Annual fee (some charge $50–$100/year to keep the line open)
- Early termination fee (some charge if you close within 2–3 years)

**Step 5: Order your documents**
Pull together your tax returns, leases, mortgage statements, and bank statements before the application. Having them ready cuts approval time.

**Step 6: Go through appraisal and underwriting**
Investment property HELOCs typically take 30–45 days to close. Plan accordingly. Don't count on this money for a deal that closes in 2 weeks.

**Step 7: Establish a draw discipline**
Once the line is open, resist the temptation to draw without a specific, underwritten purpose. A HELOC is a tool, not a slush fund. Every draw should have a corresponding ROI expectation.

## Pros and Cons for Real Estate Investors

### Pros
- **No mortgage disruption** — your first mortgage terms stay untouched (critical if you locked in a low rate)
- **Revolving access** — draw, repay, draw again during the draw period
- **Interest only during draw period** — lower carrying cost while you deploy the capital
- **Pay interest only on what you use** — unlike a cash-out refi where the full amount accrues from day one
- **Tax deductibility** — interest on a HELOC used for rental property improvements is typically deductible as a rental business expense on Schedule E [VERIFY: consult a tax advisor; IRS rules on investment HELOC deductibility are nuanced and may change]

### Cons
- **Variable rate risk** — if prime rates rise, your payment rises; model a scenario where the rate goes up 2–3 points
- **Harder to find** — limited lender options, especially for portfolios or properties in rural markets
- **Stricter qualification** — higher credit score, more reserves, more documentation than primary HELOCs
- **Can be frozen** — lenders can reduce or suspend lines if property values fall
- **Junior lien** — in a foreclosure, the HELOC lender is second; you lose the rental and the lender may take significant losses, but you still owe the balance if proceeds don't cover it

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Can I get a HELOC on a rental property I don't live in?**
Yes — that's the definition of an investment property HELOC. The property doesn't need to be owner-occupied. You will face stricter qualification requirements and fewer lender options than with a primary residence HELOC.

**Can I use a HELOC from my primary home to buy a rental?**
Yes, and this is often easier to qualify for. A HELOC on your primary residence has looser requirements (lower credit score threshold, higher LTV allowances). You can draw against it and use that cash as a down payment on an investment property. The risk is that your primary home now secures the debt — defaults would put it at risk.

**Do HELOC draws appear on my credit report?**
Yes. The HELOC will show as a revolving credit line, and the balance you draw will affect your credit utilization ratio. Plan the timing of draws relative to any other financing you'll be pursuing.

**How long does a rental property HELOC take to close?**
Plan for 30–45 days with most lenders. Specialty digital lenders may close faster (7–14 days), but that's the exception rather than the rule.

**What happens to my HELOC when I sell the rental?**
The HELOC balance must be paid off at closing. The title company will coordinate payoff of both the first mortgage and the HELOC from sale proceeds. If the proceeds don't cover both balances, you'll need to bring cash to close.

**Can I get a HELOC through my LLC?**
This is possible but uncommon. Most HELOC products are underwritten to individuals, not entities. Some private lenders will do entity-owned HELOCs, but expect higher rates and fewer choices. If entity ownership is a priority, a DSCR cash-out refinance (which does allow LLC title) may be more practical. [VERIFY: Confirm current state of LLC HELOC market with lender directory research]

## Bottom Line: Is a Rental Property HELOC Right for You?

A HELOC on a rental property is a legitimate, powerful tool — but it's not for everyone, and it's not the default choice. Here's how to think about it:

**Use a rental HELOC if:**
- You have solid equity (25%+) and a strong credit score (700+)
- You need rolling access to capital, not a one-time lump sum
- You want to preserve your existing mortgage (especially if you have a sub-6% rate)
- You have documented reserves and can service the variable payment

**Look at other options if:**
- You're self-employed with messy tax returns → DSCR cash-out refinance
- You need to close quickly → private bridge loan or hard money
- You need a lump sum and don't care about the revolving structure → cash-out refinance
- Your credit score is below 700 → focus on credit improvement first, or explore hard money
