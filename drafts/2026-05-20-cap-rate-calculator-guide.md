# Cap Rate Calculator for Real Estate Investors: How to Use It, Benchmark Your Results, and Spot When the Number Lies

A cap rate tells you how much income a property generates relative to its price — no financing, no leverage, just the property's raw earning power. The formula is: **Cap Rate = Net Operating Income (NOI) ÷ Purchase Price × 100**. A $300,000 property generating $21,000 in annual NOI has a 7% cap rate. Use the [free cap rate calculator](/calculators/cap-rate) to run any deal in under two minutes. Then come back here, because the output is only useful if you know what the number actually means.

## The Cap Rate Formula in Plain English

Cap rate has two inputs:

- **Net Operating Income (NOI)**: Annual rental income minus all operating expenses — property taxes, insurance, maintenance, management fees, and a vacancy allowance. Mortgage payments are excluded by design.
- **Purchase Price** (or current market value if you already own the property).

```
Cap Rate = NOI ÷ Purchase Price × 100
```

The reason financing is excluded is intentional: cap rate measures how the *property* performs, independent of how you paid for it. This makes it a clean, apples-to-apples comparison tool across different deals, markets, and price points. Two properties with identical 6.5% cap rates are generating the same unlevered return relative to their value, whether one investor paid cash and another put 25% down.

Once you layer in your specific financing, you get **cash-on-cash return** — a different (and equally important) metric. More on that below.

Here's a real scenario: a 3-bedroom SFR in Cleveland, OH, listed at $125,000. Current rents in the neighborhood: $1,200/month.

**Step 3 — Adjust the vacancy rate**: Cleveland's average vacancy hovers around 9-10%. Use 9%.

Effective gross income: $14,400 × 0.91 = ~$13,104

- Maintenance: $1,800/year (budget 1.5× monthly rent for a turnkey property)
- Property management: $1,310/year (10% of effective gross)
- Other: $0

Total expenses: ~$7,047

**NOI**: $13,104 − $7,047 = **$6,057**

**Cap Rate**: $6,057 ÷ $125,000 × 100 = **4.85%**

This property's asking price is high relative to local norms, and the gross rent hasn't been optimized. Before walking away, I'd model what the cap rate looks like at market rents ($1,400/month for a renovated unit) — that pushes NOI to ~$8,000 and cap rate to 6.4%. Still not great for Cleveland, but a value-add story emerges.

## Cap Rate vs. Cash-on-Cash Return vs. DSCR

These three metrics answer three different questions. You need all three before making a decision.

| Metric | Formula | Includes Financing? | Best Used For |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Cap Rate** | NOI ÷ Market Value | No | Comparing properties on an unlevered basis |
| **Cash-on-Cash Return** | Annual Cash Flow ÷ Cash Invested | Yes | Measuring actual return on your down payment |
| **DSCR** | NOI ÷ Annual Debt Service | Yes | Qualifying for a rental property loan |

**The practical workflow**:
1. Run cap rate first — it tells you whether the property is even worth analyzing further.
2. If the cap rate clears your market threshold, run [cash-on-cash return](/calculators/cash-on-cash) with your specific financing terms.
3. If you're financing with a DSCR loan, calculate your DSCR to confirm you'll qualify — most lenders require 1.20–1.25× minimum.

A property with a 7% cap rate might produce a 12% cash-on-cash return with 25% down at today's rates, or 4% with a higher loan amount on a rate buy-down. Cap rate is market-neutral; cash-on-cash is investor-specific.

## 5 Factors That Move Cap Rates in 2026

**1. The Fed rate environment.** Cap rates and bond yields historically move together, with a lag. When the 10-year Treasury yields 4–5%, investors demand more from real estate to justify the illiquidity premium. As rates rose sharply in 2022–2023, cap rates in some markets expanded 150–200 basis points. In 2025–2026, rates have stabilized, which is why cap rates have stopped expanding but haven't meaningfully compressed either. [VERIFY: current 10-year Treasury yield for accuracy]

**2. Local supply and demand.** A market with strong rent growth and low vacancy supports compressed cap rates because NOI is rising reliably. Markets with high vacancy and flat rents demand higher cap rates to compensate for uncertainty.

**3. Property condition.** A turnkey property in good condition commands a lower cap rate (better price) because buyers assign lower expense risk. Distressed or deferred-maintenance properties carry higher cap rates — partly as a risk premium, partly because buyers are discounting actual future expenses.

**4. Rental strategy.** Short-term rentals generate more gross income than long-term equivalents in the same market, which pushes effective cap rates higher — when they're operating at full performance. The complexity of managing STR revenue models means operators demand a yield premium.

**5. Property type.** Single-family rentals typically carry lower cap rates than small multifamily (more stable income, lower vacancy risk), which carry lower rates than commercial assets. The cap rate spread between asset classes in 2026 has widened as SFR institutional demand has cooled slightly while small multifamily (2–4 units) has remained strong.

## When Cap Rate Lies to You

This is the section most calculator pages skip. Cap rate is a simple formula applied to a complex reality — here are the five situations where the output will mislead you:

### 1. Short-Term Rentals with Inflated Income
STR income projections frequently use peak-season rates, 90%+ occupancy, and assume the market hasn't become oversaturated. A property showing a 12% cap rate based on Airbnb projections may print 5–6% once you use trailing 12-month actual income and account for STR regulatory risk. Always model with a full year of actuals before buying.

### 2. Value-Add Properties at Current (Depressed) NOI
A property with below-market rents, high deferred maintenance, or significant vacancy will show a cap rate based on its current distressed performance. A building with a 3% cap rate today might yield 8% once stabilized. Neither number is wrong — but you must be clear which scenario you're underwriting. Always model both "as-is" and "stabilized" cap rates, and price the gap in your offer.

### 3. Seller-Manipulated Expense Figures
Before selling, some landlords defer maintenance, lower management fees by self-managing for 90 days, or inflate income by waiving vacancy for a month. The resulting NOI — and cap rate — looks better than it is. Run your own independent expense proforma using market-rate inputs, not the seller's actuals.

### 4. Properties with Below-Market or Above-Market Rents
If a tenant has been in place for 8 years paying $850/month in a market where current rents are $1,250, the cap rate using in-place income significantly understates the property's earning potential (and vice versa). Always look at both in-place and market-rate cap rates.

### 5. Cap Rate Compression Masking Risk
In very hot markets, cap rates compress to 3–4% because buyers are paying for appreciation, not income. If that appreciation thesis doesn't play out — if the market cools or vacancy rises — you're holding a property that doesn't cash-flow. Cap rate alone doesn't tell you about the underlying assumptions baked into the price.

## Cap Rate in a BRRRR Strategy

For [BRRRR investors](/calculators/brrrr) (Buy, Rehab, Rent, Refinance, Repeat), cap rate matters twice:

**1. The entry cap rate** — what you're buying at, based on ARV and stabilized NOI after rehab. This tells you whether the deal will support a refinance at your target LTV. If the stabilized NOI on a $200k ARV property is $14,000, the cap rate is 7% — which most portfolio lenders will find acceptable.

**2. The lender's exit cap rate** — most rental property lenders (especially DSCR lenders) appraise income properties using market cap rates for their area. If you overpay on NOI projections, the appraisal may come in below your expected ARV, leaving you with a refinance gap.

The practical rule: model your BRRRR exit at a cap rate 50–100 basis points higher than you'd ideally like (i.e., be conservative on property value), and make sure your NOI covers a 1.25× DSCR at the refinance rate you expect.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**What is a good cap rate for a rental property?**
For most residential markets in 2026, a cap rate between 6–9% represents a solid cash-flow deal. Below 5% requires a strong appreciation thesis. Above 10% warrants scrutiny — verify expense assumptions and neighborhood fundamentals carefully.

**Does cap rate include mortgage payments?**
No. Cap rate is always calculated before debt service. This is by design: it lets you compare properties regardless of how each investor finances them. To factor in your specific mortgage, use [cash-on-cash return](/calculators/cash-on-cash).

**What does a 7% cap rate mean in practice?**
A 7% cap rate means the property generates annual NOI equal to 7% of its market value. On a $300,000 property, that's $21,000/year before any debt service. It also implies a rough payback period of ~14 years on an all-cash basis, though rent growth and appreciation will change that trajectory over time.

**How does cap rate relate to DSCR loan qualification?**
DSCR lenders care deeply about cap rate, indirectly. They appraise income properties using local cap rate data to determine market value, which drives their maximum loan amount. A property with a cap rate well above local norms signals higher risk to the lender; one below market norms may require a lower LTV to meet DSCR requirements. Run both metrics before applying.

**Can cap rate be used to determine property value?**
Yes — this is how commercial appraisers work. If you know the NOI and the prevailing cap rate for comparable properties, you can estimate value: Value = NOI ÷ Cap Rate. A property with $25,000 annual NOI in a 6.25% cap rate market has an implied value of $400,000. It's a starting framework, not a precise valuation.
