# DSCR Loan Requirements: The Investor's 2026 Qualification Checklist

DSCR loans qualify you based on whether the property generates enough rent to cover the mortgage — no W-2s, no tax returns, no debt-to-income calculation required. To get approved in 2026, you generally need: a DSCR ratio of 1.0 or higher, a credit score of at least 620–640, a 20–25% down payment, and 6 months of liquid reserves after closing. That's the short version. The longer version — which determines whether your specific deal actually gets funded at a rate that makes sense — is what this guide covers.

---

## What Is a DSCR Loan and Why Investors Use It

A DSCR (Debt Service Coverage Ratio) loan is a non-QM investment property mortgage where the lender underwrites based on the property's cash flow, not your personal income. The formula is simple:

**DSCR = Gross Monthly Rent ÷ Monthly PITIA**

PITIA = Principal + Interest + Taxes + Insurance + HOA (if applicable).

If a rental property generates $2,800/month in rent and the total monthly mortgage payment (including taxes and insurance) is $2,400, the DSCR is 1.17. That clears the minimum bar at most lenders.

This structure solves the core problem every serious investor eventually hits: your tax returns show low taxable income because you're doing what your accountant tells you. You depreciate everything, write off expenses, and run the numbers correctly — and then a conventional lender looks at your Schedule E and decides you can't qualify for another loan. DSCR breaks that cycle. [VERIFY: confirm this is still common lender behavior in 2026 given any potential regulatory changes]

The other reason investors prefer DSCR: there's no cap on the number of properties you can finance. Conventional Fannie/Freddie loans limit you to 10 financed properties total. DSCR lenders have no such restriction, making them the default choice for investors scaling past their first few rentals.

## The Core DSCR Loan Requirements for 2026

Here are the key qualification factors every lender will evaluate:

### 1. DSCR Ratio — The Make-or-Break Number

Your DSCR ratio determines whether you get approved, at what rate, and at what LTV.

| DSCR Ratio | What It Means | Typical Lender Response |
|---|---|---|
| 1.25 or higher | Strong positive cash flow | Best rates, max LTV (up to 80%), easiest approval |
| 1.00–1.24 | Property breaks even or slightly positive | Standard approval, slightly higher rate |
| 0.75–0.99 | Property loses money monthly | Specialty "no-ratio" programs; expect 30–35% down, materially higher rate |
| Below 0.75 | Significant negative cash flow | Most lenders decline; hard money or portfolio lenders only |

The sweet spot most experienced investors target before submitting an application is 1.15 or above. That buffer gives you room for the appraisal to come in slightly below your expected rent without killing the deal.

**The appraisal risk most investors overlook:** Your DSCR calculation uses the rent the appraiser assigns on Form 1007, not the rent you're projecting. If you've run your numbers assuming $2,500/month but the appraiser's market rent analysis comes back at $2,200, your DSCR just dropped. If that drops you below 1.0, you may need to renegotiate the purchase price or increase your down payment to lower the monthly debt service.

Practical fix: before you commit to a purchase price, run your deal using [our rental cash flow calculator](/calculators/rental-cashflow) at 85–90% of your projected rent, not 100%. If the deal still works at the conservative rent estimate, your appraisal risk is manageable.

### 2. Credit Score Requirements

Because DSCR lenders skip income verification, your credit history becomes their primary signal of borrower reliability. The technical minimums vary by lender, but here's how credit score tiers translate to actual loan terms:

| Credit Score | LTV Available | Rate Impact |
|---|---|---|
| 740+ | Up to 80% (max) | Best available rate |
| 700–739 | Typically 75–80% | Standard rate |
| 660–699 | Usually 70–75% | Rate premium of ~0.25–0.5% |
| 620–659 | 65–70% LTV cap at many lenders | Rate premium of 0.5–1.5%; fewer lender options |
| Below 620 | Very few lenders; niche programs only | Significantly higher rate |

Most lenders in our [DSCR lender directory](/lenders/dscr-loans) start at 620–640. A few, like RCN Capital, go as low as 620 — useful if your credit took a hit during a previous downturn and you're rebuilding. If you're at 660, paying down revolving balances to get above 700 before you apply is often worth a few weeks' delay — the rate improvement over 30 years more than offsets any acquisition timing cost.

One nuance conventional-loan borrowers often miss: bankruptcies and foreclosures have a shorter waiting period for DSCR loans (typically 3–4 years) compared to conventional mortgages (typically 7 years). This makes DSCR the primary path back to portfolio growth for investors who went through a rough patch in 2008–2012 or during COVID.

### 3. Down Payment and LTV Requirements

DSCR loans require real equity. Unlike FHA or conventional owner-occupied loans, there's no 3.5% or 5% down path here.

- **Purchase:** Most lenders require 20–25% down (80–75% LTV)
- **Cash-out refinance:** Max LTV typically 70–75%; lenders are more conservative when you're pulling equity
- **Rate-and-term refinance:** Can often go to 80% LTV depending on the lender

The down payment can come from:
- Cash savings (most straightforward)
- Cash-out refinance proceeds from another property you already own
- Gift funds (some lenders allow this; verify before assuming)
- Business accounts (acceptable at most lenders)
- Cross-collateralization against another owned property (not universally offered)

The BRRRR strategy intersects directly here: investors use short-term hard money or bridge financing to acquire and rehab a property, then refinance into a DSCR loan once it's stabilized with a tenant in place. At that point, if the forced appreciation from the rehab is sufficient, you can refinance out most or all of your initial equity — effectively recycling your capital into the next deal. See our [lender directory](/lenders/dscr-loans) for lenders that offer both fix-and-flip and DSCR products, which simplifies the BRRRR refinance.

### 4. Reserve Requirements — The Requirement Most Investors Forget

This is the requirement that blindsides the most first-time DSCR borrowers: you can't show up at closing with exactly enough cash for the down payment and fees.

Most lenders require **6 months of PITIA in liquid reserves** after closing. That means if your total monthly mortgage payment (including taxes and insurance) is $2,000, you need $12,000 sitting in a verifiable account after you wire the down payment.

Acceptable reserve sources:
- Personal or business checking/savings accounts (verified with 2 months of statements)
- Investment or brokerage accounts (typically counted at 70% of vested value)
- 401(k) or IRA assets (often counted at 60–70% after early withdrawal penalties are factored)
- Proceeds from another property sale (with documentation)

### 5. Property Type Eligibility

Not all properties qualify under standard DSCR programs:

**Standard eligibility (most lenders):**
- Single-family homes (SFR)
- Condominiums (check warrantability requirements)
- Townhomes
- 2–4 unit multifamily (duplex, triplex, fourplex)

**Specialty eligibility (select lenders):**
- Short-term rentals / Airbnb properties (see STR section below)
- Rural properties (acreage limitations vary)
- Non-warrantable condos (niche programs, higher rate)
- 5+ unit multifamily (typically falls under commercial multifamily underwriting, not residential DSCR)

**Properties that typically don't qualify:**
- Mixed-use commercial
- Properties with active code violations
- Land-only purchases
- Mobile homes on leased land

## Short-Term Rentals and DSCR Loans

The STR/Airbnb DSCR market has matured significantly. Several lenders now offer purpose-built STR DSCR programs that use projected short-term rental income to calculate the qualifying ratio.

How it works: If there's no long-term lease in place, the appraiser uses AirDNA market data (or similar platforms) to estimate market short-term rental income for the property. Lenders typically apply a 10–20% discount to this estimate before running the DSCR calculation — so if AirDNA projects $4,200/month, the lender might underwrite to $3,400–$3,780.

Before you commit to a vacation rental acquisition, run your numbers at the conservatively discounted figure. If the deal works at 80% of projected AirDNA income, you have a viable DSCR candidate. ## The Documentation You'll Actually Need

The beauty of DSCR is the short document list compared to a conventional loan. Here's what to prepare:

**Required at all lenders:**
- Standard 1003 loan application
- Government-issued ID
- Most recent 2 months of bank statements (to verify down payment and reserves)
- Property insurance quote or binder (needed before closing)
- Appraisal with Form 1007 Rent Schedule (lender orders this)

**If closing in an LLC (recommended for most investors):**
- Articles of Organization
- Operating Agreement
- EIN documentation
- Sometimes: certificate of good standing from the state

**If using a short-term rental income:**
- AirDNA or similar market rental data
- Prior platform income history (if the property is already operating)

No W-2s. No tax returns. No personal profit-and-loss statements. No DTI calculation.

## Loan Terms: What You're Getting Into

Understanding the structure helps you make the right financing decision:

**Common term options:**
- 30-year fixed (most common, most stable)
- 5/6 or 7/6 ARM (lower initial rate; makes sense if you plan to sell or refi within 5–7 years)
- 40-year fixed or interest-only (lower monthly payment = higher DSCR ratio; useful for marginal deals)
- Interest-only 10-year period with 30-year amortization

**Prepayment penalties:** Standard in DSCR loans. Common structures:
- 3-2-1 step-down: 3% penalty in year 1, 2% in year 2, 1% in year 3, then none
- 5-4-3-2-1 step-down: More aggressive; used when lenders offer lower rates in exchange

If you're executing a BRRRR strategy with a planned refinance within 2–3 years, pay close attention to prepayment penalties. The math of refinancing into a lower-rate environment can get wiped out by a 3–5% penalty on the outstanding balance.

Before you submit an application to any DSCR lender, verify you can answer "yes" to each of these:

## Common DSCR Disqualifiers (and How to Fix Them)

| Issue | Why It's a Problem | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| DSCR below 1.0 | Deal loses money monthly | Lower purchase price, increase down payment, negotiate concessions |
| Credit score under 640 | Limits lender options significantly | Pay down revolving balances; dispute errors; wait 60–90 days |
| Insufficient reserves | Fails post-closing liquidity test | Delay application until reserves are accumulated; use proceeds from other account |

| STR in a restricted market | HOA or local ordinance limits short-term use | Verify local STR permitting before buying; get legal opinion if unclear |
| LLC not formed | Application hold while state processes paperwork | Form LLC 4–6 weeks before expected application date |

## Is a DSCR Loan Right for Your Deal?

DSCR loans make the most sense when:
- Your personal income is low on paper due to business deductions and depreciation
- You've already used your conventional loan limit (10 properties)
- You're self-employed with inconsistent or complex income documentation
- You want to close in an LLC for liability protection
- You're scaling a rental portfolio and need the flexibility to close multiple deals per year

They make less sense when:
- Your personal income qualifies easily and you can get a conventional rate (which is typically 1–2% lower)
- You're buying your first investment property with strong W-2 income and clean finances
- The deal only works at an 8%+ interest rate, which may compress returns to the point where alternatives make more sense
