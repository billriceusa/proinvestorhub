# Cap Rate Calculator: What the Number Really Means for Rental Property Investors

A cap rate calculator tells you what percentage of a property's value it earns each year as net income — before financing. Enter your purchase price and net operating income into our [free cap rate calculator](/calculators/cap-rate) and you get a number in seconds. But that number alone won't tell you whether to buy the deal. This guide explains what a good cap rate looks like, how it changes by market and strategy, and four situations where investors who rely on cap rate alone get burned.

---

## The Cap Rate Formula

Cap rate has two inputs:

**Cap Rate = Net Operating Income (NOI) ÷ Purchase Price × 100**

- **NOI** is annual gross rent minus operating expenses (property taxes, insurance, maintenance, management fees, vacancy allowance). Mortgage payments do NOT go here — cap rate is always calculated before debt service.
- **Purchase price** is what you paid, or current market value if you already own the property.

That's it. A $200,000 property generating $14,000 in NOI has a 7% cap rate.

The reason cap rate excludes financing is intentional: it lets you compare two properties on equal footing regardless of how each is financed. Use it to filter deals. Use [cash-on-cash return](/calculators/cash-on-cash) once you know your loan terms.

## Step-by-Step: How to Calculate Cap Rate

Let's run a real example. A three-bedroom house in Indianapolis:

| Line Item | Monthly | Annual |
|---|---|---|
| Gross rent | $1,350 | $16,200 |
| Vacancy (5%) | −$68 | −$810 |
| Property taxes | −$125 | −$1,500 |
| Insurance | −$80 | −$960 |
| Maintenance/CapEx reserve | −$135 | −$1,620 |
| Property management (8%) | −$108 | −$1,296 |
| **Net Operating Income** | **$834** | **$10,014** |

Purchase price: $175,000

**Cap Rate = $10,014 ÷ $175,000 × 100 = 5.7%**

Wait — Indianapolis averages 8.1% cap rates on our [markets page](/calculators/cap-rate/cities). Why is this deal under that?

Two reasons: First, the asking price may be above the market average. Second, this house may be in a higher-demand neighborhood where sellers price for appreciation. ## What Is a Good Cap Rate?

There is no universal answer. "Good" depends on your market, your strategy, and what you're trying to build. Here's the investor's framework:

| Cap Rate | Typical Market | What It Generally Signals |
|---|---|---|
| 3–5% | Gateway cities (NYC, LA, SF, Seattle) | Low yield, strong appreciation play, lower vacancy risk |
| 5–7% | Sunbelt metros, suburban secondaries | Balanced return — can work for both cash flow and appreciation |
| 7–9% | Midwest, South, tertiary markets | Cash-flow focused, higher management intensity |
| 9%+ | Distressed areas, high-vacancy markets | High yield compensates for real risks; not passive |

**The one rule that matters more than any table:** your cap rate must clear your cost of capital. If you're financing at a 7.5% interest rate on a DSCR loan, a 6% cap rate means the property is cash-flow negative from day one. ## Cap Rate by Investment Strategy

Not every investor should be targeting the same cap rate. Here's how the number fits into four common strategies:

### Buy-and-Hold (Long-Term Rental)

Target range: 6–8% in secondary markets, 7–10% in tertiary markets.

You need enough yield to cover operating expenses, debt service on a DSCR or conventional loan, and still produce monthly cash flow. The [rental cash flow calculator](/calculators/rental-cashflow) helps model this once you have your cap rate.

### BRRRR (Buy, Rehab, Rent, Refinance, Repeat)

Cap rate matters most at the refinance stage, not the purchase. When you refinance a rehabbed property, lenders use the stabilized NOI to determine your new loan amount. A higher post-rehab cap rate means more equity to pull.

The BRRRR math: if you buy a distressed property for $80K, put $25K into it, and the stabilized value is $150K at an 8% cap rate — your NOI is $12,000 and your refinance to 75% LTV yields $112,500. You've recycled most of your capital. The [BRRRR calculator](/calculators/brrrr) models the full cycle.

### Short-Term / Vacation Rental

STR cap rates often look higher on paper (8–14%) but the NOI is less predictable. Platforms like Airbnb have variable occupancy, and operating expenses are significantly higher than long-term rental. Use the [STR revenue estimator](/calculators/str-revenue) to stress-test occupancy assumptions before trusting a high cap rate figure on an STR property.

### Value-Add (Repositioning)

Don't use the *current* cap rate — use the *stabilized* cap rate you're underwriting after improvements and lease-up. A property at 4.5% cap rate today that pencils out at 7.5% after $40K in improvements and a rent increase from $1,100 to $1,600 is a fundamentally different deal than its asking price suggests.

## Cap Rate vs. Cash-on-Cash Return vs. DSCR

These three metrics each answer a different question. Most experienced investors run all three before committing.

| Metric | Formula | Includes Financing? | Best Used For |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Cap Rate** | NOI ÷ Market Value × 100 | No | Comparing properties, benchmarking market |
| **Cash-on-Cash Return** | Annual Pre-Tax Cash Flow ÷ Cash Invested × 100 | Yes | Measuring actual return on your invested dollars |
| **DSCR** | NOI ÷ Annual Debt Service | Yes | Qualifying for a DSCR loan; checking coverage margin |
| **Gross Rent Multiplier** | Market Value ÷ Gross Annual Rent | No | Fast first screen, not for serious underwriting |

Cap rate and cash-on-cash are complementary, not competing. Run cap rate to filter the deal pool. Run cash-on-cash to decide if your specific financing makes a specific deal work.

If you're financing with a DSCR loan (increasingly the default for investor properties), your lender will calculate DSCR from the same NOI you used for cap rate. Most DSCR lenders require a minimum 1.20–1.25 DSCR. Use our [lender finder](/lenders/finder) to compare DSCR lenders by rate and requirements. ## 4 Situations Where Cap Rate Alone Gets Investors in Trouble

Cap rate is a useful filter, not a verdict. Here are four scenarios where a good cap rate disguises a bad deal — and one where a "bad" cap rate can be the right call.

**1. You're ignoring property taxes in high-rate states.**
Detroit's 2.76% property tax rate quietly destroys cap rates that look high on paper. A property with $12,000 gross NOI in Michigan loses $2,346 in annual taxes alone. Always calculate NOI with *accurate local tax rates*, not a national average plug.

**2. You're using gross rent, not stabilized net rent.**
A seller presenting a "10% cap rate" may be using gross rent without deducting management fees, vacancy, or CapEx reserves. Real-world NOI for a Class C property is often 35–45% of gross rent, not the 50% you might assume.

**3. You're buying appreciation at a 3% cap rate without a plan.**
A 3.8% cap rate in Seattle means a $500K property earns $19,000 in NOI. At 7% financing on 75% LTV, your annual debt service is $22,400. You're cash-flow negative by $3,400 per year. This can still be the right trade if you have conviction on long-term appreciation and can carry the loss — but you have to go in with eyes open.

**4. You're calculating cap rate off the wrong basis.**
If you bought a property three years ago for $200K and it's now worth $350K, your original 8% cap rate (based on $200K) is now a 4.6% cap rate on current value. This matters if you're deciding whether to refinance, 1031, or hold.

**The flip side:** A 4.5% cap rate in a market with 5% annual rent growth can outperform a 9% cap rate in a flat market over a 10-year hold. Cap rate is a snapshot. Compounding rent growth rewrites the story.

## Using Cap Rate to Qualify for Financing

If you plan to use a DSCR loan — now the most common financing structure for investor properties — your cap rate calculation IS your lender's underwriting starting point.

DSCR = NOI ÷ Annual Debt Service

A property with $14,000 NOI and a $11,000 annual mortgage payment has a 1.27 DSCR. Most DSCR lenders require 1.20–1.25 minimum.

The practical implication: a property's cap rate needs to be meaningfully higher than your effective interest rate, or the DSCR math won't work. At a 7.5% rate on a 30-year DSCR loan at 75% LTV, a $300K purchase requires roughly $19,125 in annual debt service. Your NOI needs to be at least $23,906 (1.25 × debt service) — implying a minimum cap rate of around 8% on that property and those loan terms.
