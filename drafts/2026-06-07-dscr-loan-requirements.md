# DSCR Loan Requirements: The Investor's Complete Qualification Guide (2026)

A DSCR loan qualifies you based on what the property earns — not what you earn. To qualify in 2026, most lenders require a minimum DSCR ratio of 1.0 (rents cover the mortgage), a credit score of 620–680+, a 20–25% down payment, and 3–6 months of cash reserves. The exact numbers vary by lender — sometimes dramatically — and understanding those differences is where the real work happens.

This guide covers every qualification factor, real deal examples with current-rate math, the red flags that kill applications, and when a DSCR loan is the wrong tool for the job. Unlike pages written by lenders who want you to close with them, this guide is written for investors who want to shop intelligently.

---

## What Makes a DSCR Loan Different

Before we get into requirements, one sentence is worth understanding: DSCR lenders don't care how much you make — they care how much the *property* makes.

That's the entire premise. No W-2s, no tax returns, no debt-to-income ratio tied to your personal income. The lender divides the property's gross monthly rent by the total monthly mortgage payment (principal, interest, taxes, insurance, and any HOA fees). The resulting ratio is your DSCR.

**DSCR = Gross Monthly Rent ÷ Monthly PITIA**

- DSCR of 1.25 → Property generates 25% more than the mortgage payment. Lenders love this.
- DSCR of 1.0 → Property breaks even. Most lenders will accept this.
- DSCR of 0.85 → Negative cash flow. A narrower set of lenders will go here, but rates and down payment requirements increase.

That's the core logic. Everything else flows from it.

## The 7 Core DSCR Loan Requirements

### 1. Minimum DSCR Ratio

Most lenders set their floor at 1.0, meaning rents must cover the full mortgage payment. Some offer "no-ratio" or sub-1.0 programs for properties with strong appreciation potential or in markets where rent growth is expected, but plan on higher rates and larger down payments if you need one of those.

Where it gets interesting: lenders also use DSCR as a pricing lever. A property at 1.25 DSCR will get a better rate than one at 1.0, even with the same borrower credit profile. Run the numbers before negotiating — a rent increase of $100/month can meaningfully shift your rate tier.

**What to know for 2026:** Rising property taxes and insurance costs in high-risk markets (Florida, parts of Texas, California coastal) are squeezing DSCRs that looked fine 18–24 months ago. Insurance premiums on a $400K SFR can now run $4,000–$8,000/year in some counties, which adds $333–$667/month to your PITIA. Factor this into your calculation before assuming a property qualifies. ### 2. Credit Score Requirements

Credit still matters. Lenders aren't looking at your income, but they are looking at how you handle debt. Credit score affects three things: whether you're approved at all, what rate you get, and how much down payment is required.

General ranges across the [DSCR lenders we review](/lenders/dscr-loans):

| Credit Score Range | Typical Outcome |
|---|---|
| 720+ | Best rates, lowest down payment requirements, maximum flexibility |
| 680–719 | Standard pricing; most programs fully available |
| 640–679 | Approved at most lenders, but rates step up and some programs close off |
| 620–639 | Fewer lender options; lower LTV required at most; RCN Capital and similar go here |
| Below 620 | Hard money / bridge territory; standard DSCR programs generally unavailable |

The 660–680 threshold is where the biggest pricing cliffs appear. If you're at 672, it's worth understanding whether a targeted credit improvement could save you 0.25–0.50% on a long-term loan — on a $350K loan over 30 years, that's meaningful.

### 3. Down Payment and LTV

DSCR loans are investment-only products, and lenders require more skin in the game than you'd need for a primary residence. Standard parameters:

- **Purchase:** 20–25% down (75–80% LTV)
- **Cash-out refinance:** 70–75% LTV (you retain more equity)
- **Rate/term refinance:** Up to 80% LTV

Some lenders advertise 85% LTV programs, but these typically carry significant rate premiums and tighter DSCR requirements. For most investors building a rental portfolio, targeting 75–80% LTV produces the best long-term economics.

**Down payment sourcing matters.** Lenders will review 30–60 days of bank statements to confirm your down payment is "seasoned" — meaning it's been sitting in your account, not deposited last week from a personal loan or gift. Moving money around before an application can trigger underwriting flags.

One legitimate strategy many investors use: a DSCR cash-out refinance on an existing property funds the down payment on a new acquisition. The equity from property A becomes the down payment for property B. Use our [rental cash flow calculator](/calculators/rental-cashflow) to model whether the new payment structure on property A still pencils out after the cash-out.

### 4. Cash Reserves

After you close, lenders want to see a buffer — money that stays in place to cover mortgage payments if the property sits vacant or a roof fails. Typical requirements: 3–6 months of PITIA per property, liquid.

"Liquid" is the key word. Retirement accounts may count at 60–70% of face value; equity in other properties generally does not. If you're closing on multiple properties simultaneously (a portfolio purchase, for example), some lenders will want reserves for the entire portfolio.

Higher reserve requirements apply if:
- Your DSCR is below 1.10
- Your credit score is below 680
- The property is a short-term rental (STR programs often require 6+ months of reserves)

### 5. Appraisal and Rent Schedule

DSCR appraisals have a piece that standard residential appraisals don't: the rent schedule. Appraisers use Form 1007 for single-family properties (or Form 1025 for 2–4 units) to document comparable rents in the market and estimate what the property should realistically command.

This matters because the lender will use whichever is **lower** — the actual lease amount or the appraiser's market rent estimate. If your tenant is paying above-market rent, you don't get full credit for it. Conversely, if the property is vacant, the appraisal's market rent estimate becomes the qualifying figure.

Properties must be in rentable condition. Significant deferred maintenance or structural issues will either kill the DSCR appraisal outright or require repairs before funding. If you're buying a distressed property that needs work before it can be leased, a DSCR loan is the wrong tool — you want a [hard money loan](/lenders/hard-money-loans) or bridge loan for the acquisition/rehab phase, then refinance into DSCR once the property is stabilized.

### 6. Property Type and Use

DSCR loans are limited to:
- 1–4 unit residential investment properties (single-family, townhomes, condos, duplexes through fourplexes)
- Non-owner-occupied only — you cannot use DSCR financing on your primary residence
- Warrantable and non-warrantable condos (guidelines vary; ask specifically)

What's generally NOT eligible:
- 5+ unit properties (these are commercial loans — different product, different underwriting)
- Owner-occupied properties
- Properties requiring major renovation before occupancy

**Short-term rentals (STR/Airbnb):** Many lenders now offer STR-eligible DSCR programs, but underwriting differs. Some lenders use market rent (what a long-term lease would bring); others accept 12-month STR income history from AirDNA or platform data. STR programs frequently require higher DSCR thresholds and additional reserves given income volatility. If your exit strategy depends on STR income, confirm explicitly that the lender you're targeting uses a methodology favorable to your property.

### 7. Entity Structure

One of the most underrated benefits of DSCR loans: you can close in an LLC or other business entity. This is important for asset protection — it separates your rental portfolio's liability from your personal assets.

Most lenders allow LLC ownership with a personal guarantee from the principal. Some will extend to trust structures; fewer will accept entities without a personal guarantee. Confirm entity type early in the process because it affects title, closing procedures, and some underwriting requirements.

## Red Flags That Kill DSCR Applications

These are the things lenders don't always tell you upfront. Knowing them before you apply saves time and unnecessary credit pulls.

**1. Recently purchased down payment funds.** If your 25% down appeared in your account in the last 30–60 days, underwriters will ask where it came from. Personal loans, credit card cash advances, or undisclosed gifts will trigger repaper requests or denials.

**2. Properties in high-insurance-cost counties.** In some Florida, Louisiana, and California counties, annual insurance now exceeds $10,000 on a $400K property. These costs can push an otherwise-qualifying DSCR below 1.0. Get insurance quotes before you're in contract.

**3. Condo projects with deferred maintenance or high commercial space.** Condos in projects that fail Fannie warrantability guidelines (more than 35% commercial space, 15%+ of units delinquent on HOA dues, etc.) may not be eligible for standard DSCR programs, even though DSCR isn't an agency product. Ask lenders specifically about their condo approval process.

**4. Below-market leases.** If you're buying a property with a long-term tenant paying $1,400/month in a market where rent is $1,850, the appraiser will use the market rent — but if the tenant's lease runs two more years, that's a DSCR calculation based on income you won't see for 24 months. Some lenders will account for lease roll; others use only what's current.

**5. Properties in LLC with complex ownership.** Multiple-member LLCs, trusts, and operating agreements with unusual provisions can slow or stall closings. Have your entity documents ready from day one.

## When a DSCR Loan Is NOT the Right Choice

Lenders won't tell you this. We will.

**You're buying a property that needs significant renovation.** DSCR loans fund stabilized, rentable properties. If the property needs a new roof, HVAC replacement, or significant rehab before a tenant can move in, get hard money or a bridge loan for the acquisition/rehab phase. Once stabilized, refinance into DSCR. [The BRRRR calculator](/calculators/brrrr) helps model the full sequence — acquisition, rehab, ARV appraisal, and long-term DSCR refinance.

**You qualify easily for conventional financing and have fewer than 10 properties.** Conventional investment property loans carry lower rates than DSCR (typically 0.5–1.5% lower). If you have strong W-2 income, a clean DTI, and fewer than 10 financed properties, conventional may be the smarter first-cost option. Run the math: the rate premium on DSCR adds real money over a 30-year hold.

**You need 90%+ LTV.** DSCR caps at 80% in most programs. If you need higher leverage (less cash to close), a fix-and-flip loan or seller financing may be more appropriate tools.

**The property's market rent doesn't cover the mortgage.** Negative DSCR programs exist, but they're expensive and limited. If the rental economics genuinely don't work at current rates and rents, that's a deal problem, not a financing problem.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Can I get a DSCR loan with no money down?**
No. Unlike FHA or VA loans for primary residences, DSCR loans require a minimum 20% down payment. The equity requirement compensates for the absence of personal income verification.

**Do DSCR lenders verify my income at all?**
They don't use your income to qualify you. But they may review bank statements to verify your down payment and reserves are legitimate and properly seasoned.

**Can I use projected rent, or does a tenant need to be in place?**
Projected market rent from the appraiser is acceptable — you don't need a signed lease. However, a lease in place at or above market rent removes ambiguity and can speed up the appraisal process.

**What's the difference between DSCR and a conventional investment property loan?**
Conventional loans use your personal debt-to-income ratio and require W-2s, tax returns, and pay stubs. They cap you at 10 financed properties and generally offer lower rates. DSCR loans use only the property's income, have no property-count limit, and cost 0.5–1.5% more in rate. For a full comparison, see our [DSCR loans guide](/blog/dscr-loans-explained-complete-guide-real-estate-investors-2026).

**Can I close a DSCR loan in my LLC?**
Yes. This is one of DSCR's key advantages over conventional investment loans — you can take title in a business entity for liability protection. A personal guarantee from the member(s) is typically still required.

**What happens if my DSCR is below 1.0?**
Some lenders offer negative-DSCR or no-ratio programs, but they require higher down payments (30–35%), charge higher rates, and are available only at a subset of lenders. If the deal requires a sub-1.0 DSCR program to work, stress-test the math carefully — you're essentially funding a property that doesn't cover its own debt from day one.
