# DSCR Loan Pros and Cons: An Investor-First Guide (With a Decision Framework)

A DSCR loan lets you qualify for rental property financing based on the property's income — not your W-2, tax returns, or personal debt-to-income ratio. If the property cashflows, you can likely get the loan. That's the core pitch. But DSCR loans also carry higher rates, larger down payment requirements, and prepayment penalties that can erode returns. This guide breaks down every pro and con with real numbers so you can decide whether a DSCR loan is right for your next deal.

---

## What Is a DSCR Loan?

DSCR stands for Debt Service Coverage Ratio — a single number that tells a lender whether your rental property generates enough income to cover its own mortgage payments.

**The formula:**

> **DSCR = Monthly Gross Rental Income ÷ Monthly PITIA**
>
> (PITIA = Principal + Interest + Taxes + Insurance + HOA, if any)

A DSCR of 1.0 means the property breaks even on debt service. A DSCR of 1.25 means rent covers the mortgage with 25% cushion. Most lenders want at least 1.0, with the best terms reserved for 1.25 and above.

Because approval is based on the property's income — not yours — DSCR loans are classified as Non-QM (non-qualified mortgage) products. That classification has real consequences for rate and terms, which we'll cover below.

### A Quick DSCR Calculation Example

Say you're buying a single-family rental in Memphis:

| Item | Monthly |
|---|---|
| Market rent | $1,850 |
| PITIA (estimated) | $1,440 |
| **DSCR** | **$1,850 ÷ $1,440 = 1.28** |

## DSCR Loan Pros

### 1. No Personal Income Verification

This is the reason most investors choose DSCR financing. You don't submit W-2s, pay stubs, or tax returns. The underwriter evaluates the property, not you.

This matters in several specific scenarios:
- **Self-employed investors** whose tax returns show lower income after deductions than they actually earn
- **Investors with multiple properties** whose DTI is maxed out under conventional guidelines
- **High-earner investors** with irregular income (commission, K-1, rental income that isn't yet seasoned)

Conventional financing via Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac typically requires two years of documented income, a DTI under 45%, and counts each property's mortgage payment against that DTI. DSCR loans sidestep all of that.

### 2. Close in an LLC or Entity

Most DSCR lenders allow — and many prefer — that you take title in an LLC or other business entity. Conventional mortgages from Fannie/Freddie require individual-name ownership.

This matters for:
- **Liability protection** — separating rental property from personal assets
- **Estate planning** — easier to transfer ownership interests
- **Scaling** — adding properties under a single entity with cleaner books

### 3. No Cap on Number of Properties

Fannie Mae conventional loans cap you at 10 financed properties. After that, most conventional lenders won't touch your application regardless of credit or income.

DSCR loans have no such ceiling. An investor with 25 rental properties can add a 26th using DSCR financing as long as that property pencils. This is the most significant structural advantage for investors building a portfolio past the single-digit mark.

### 4. Faster Underwriting

Because DSCR underwriting is property-focused, the documentation package is thinner. No income verification means less back-and-forth with the underwriter. Many DSCR lenders can close in 21–30 days versus 45–60 days for conventional investment property loans.

Speed matters in competitive markets where sellers favor cash offers or short contingency windows.

### 5. Works Well for the BRRRR Refinance Step

If you're executing the [BRRRR strategy](/calculators/brrrr) (Buy, Rehab, Rent, Refinance, Repeat), the refinance step is where DSCR loans shine. After you've stabilized the property with a tenant in place, you refinance using the property's rental income — pulling out equity to fund the next deal. A conventional cash-out refinance on an investment property hits DTI and income limits fast. DSCR cash-out refinances don't.

### 6. Short-Term Rental (STR) Eligible

Several DSCR lenders will accept projected Airbnb/VRBO income — sometimes using AirDNA comps — rather than requiring an existing long-term lease. This opens DSCR financing to STR investors who don't yet have 12 months of rental history on a property.

## DSCR Loan Cons

### 1. Higher Interest Rates

DSCR loans price at a meaningful premium over conventional investment property mortgages. As of mid-2026, DSCR rates are running roughly 6.75%–8.75% depending on DSCR ratio, LTV, credit score, and prepayment penalty selection. Conventional investment property loans from Fannie/Freddie (for investors who still qualify) are pricing around 7.0%–7.5% — so the gap has narrowed, but it still exists and adds up over a 30-year hold. [VERIFY: confirm current rate ranges with lenders in directory; market conditions change rapidly]

**The practical impact:** On a $300,000 loan, a 1.25% rate premium adds approximately $230/month in interest cost. That's real money to model before committing.

### 2. Larger Down Payment Required

DSCR lenders typically require 20–25% down. A few lenders will go to 15% down on strong DSCR properties, but rates rise sharply. There is no 5% or 10% down option here — DSCR is not a low-down-payment product.

This is a meaningful capital commitment. A $350,000 rental at 25% down requires $87,500 at close before closing costs.

### 3. Prepayment Penalties Are Standard

Most DSCR loans include a prepayment penalty, typically structured as a step-down over 3–5 years:

| Year | Penalty |
|---|---|
| Year 1 | 5% of outstanding balance |
| Year 2 | 4% |
| Year 3 | 3% |
| Year 4 | 2% |
| Year 5 | 1% |

If you sell or refinance in year 2, you pay 4% of the loan balance. On a $300,000 loan, that's $12,000.

Some lenders offer prepayment-penalty-free DSCR loans, but they price the rate 0.5%–1.0% higher to compensate. If you plan to hold long-term (5+ years), taking the penalty in exchange for a lower rate usually wins. If you might sell in 2–3 years, consider the no-PPP option.

### 4. Minimum DSCR Thresholds Can Kill Deals in Low-Yield Markets

In high-cost markets — coastal metros, major urban cores — rent-to-price ratios are thin. A $700,000 San Diego rental that rents for $3,200/month may have a DSCR below 1.0. Most DSCR lenders won't finance a property below a 1.0 DSCR, and competitive pricing typically requires 1.15+.

This is a structural constraint. DSCR loans are best suited to markets where rent yields support the ratio — which correlates strongly with markets that also show strong cash-on-cash returns. Use the [Cap Rate Calculator](/calculators/cap-rate) to evaluate whether a target market is DSCR-viable before underwriting a deal.

### 5. Reserve Requirements Add to Capital Commitment

Many lenders require 6–12 months of PITIA in liquid reserves at closing. On a $1,440/month payment, that's $8,640–$17,280 sitting in a bank account that you can't deploy to the next deal.

Between the down payment, closing costs (~2–4% of loan amount), and reserve requirements, a single DSCR acquisition can tie up $120,000–$140,000 for a $350,000 property.

### 6. Higher Origination Fees

DSCR lenders frequently charge 1–2 points in origination fees versus 0–0.5 points on conventional investment loans. This adds to acquisition cost and slightly increases the return hurdle the deal must clear.

## When DSCR Makes Sense vs. When It Doesn't

Use this framework to decide before running the numbers:

### DSCR Is a Strong Fit When:

- You're self-employed or have W-2 income that doesn't reflect your full financial picture
- You already have 4+ financed conventional loans and are approaching the 10-property Fannie/Freddie limit
- You're buying in an LLC from day one for liability protection
- The property has a clear rental history or strong comparable rents (DSCR ≥ 1.15)
- You plan to hold the property for 5+ years (prepayment penalty is negligible in NPV terms)
- You're executing a BRRRR cash-out refinance after stabilizing a property

### Consider Alternatives When:

- You qualify for a conventional investment property loan — conventional rates are competitive and there's no prepayment penalty
- Your property's rent barely covers the mortgage (DSCR near 1.0) — a market dip or vacancy could stress the loan
- You plan to sell or refinance within 3 years — the prepayment penalty could wipe out a year's cash flow
- You need low down payment — DSCR doesn't solve that problem; look at FHA house-hack strategies or portfolio lenders instead
- You're buying a property to rehab (not yet rentable) — DSCR requires an income-producing property at the time of closing; use a hard money or bridge loan for the acquisition/rehab phase, then DSCR to refinance out

## What DSCR Lenders Actually Look At (Beyond the Ratio)

The DSCR ratio is the headline, but underwriters also evaluate:

**Credit score:** Most DSCR lenders require a minimum 620–640. Below 680, expect rate add-ons of 0.5%–1.0%. Above 740, you'll access the best pricing.

**LTV (Loan-to-Value):** Lower LTV = better rate. The pricing sweet spot is typically 75% LTV or lower.

**Property type:** Single-family rentals (SFR) and 2–4 unit properties get the best rates. 5+ unit multifamily is commercial and typically requires a different DSCR lender. Short-term rentals may face lender overlays.

**Rent comps vs. actual leases:** Some lenders accept market rent projections from an appraiser; others require an executed lease and 12 months of rental history. Know your lender's requirements before you get under contract.

**Reserves:** Typically 3–12 months of PITIA in liquid assets post-close.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**What is the minimum DSCR to get a loan?**
Most lenders set a floor of 1.0, meaning the property must at least break even on debt service. A few lenders (typically those targeting distressed-asset specialists) will go as low as 0.75–0.80, but rates are significantly higher. Aim for 1.15+ for competitive pricing.

**Can I use projected rent if the property is vacant?**
Some lenders will accept a market rent appraisal from a licensed appraiser in lieu of an executed lease. This is lender-specific — many require an active lease. Confirm before going under contract.

**Do DSCR loans show up on my personal credit?**
Yes. Even though approval doesn't require personal income verification, most DSCR lenders still pull a personal credit report and the loan will appear on your personal credit file. If you want the debt off your personal credit, ask about business-purpose DSCR loans underwritten solely to the entity — a smaller subset of lenders offers these.

**Can I get a DSCR loan for a short-term rental?**
Yes, but with caveats. Lenders who accept STR income typically use AirDNA or market comps to estimate income — not your personal Airbnb dashboard. Some lenders apply a conservative occupancy haircut (e.g., 75% of projected gross). Confirm with the lender how they underwrite STR income before building your pro forma.

**Is DSCR better than hard money for a rental property?**
For a stabilized, tenant-occupied rental with a holding period of 5+ years, DSCR is almost always the better option — lower rates, longer terms, and no call features. Hard money is better for acquisition/rehab situations where you need to close fast on a property that doesn't yet qualify for DSCR underwriting.

## The Bottom Line

DSCR loans are the most scalable financing product available to individual rental property investors. The ability to qualify based solely on property income — with no personal income verification, no property limit, and LLC eligibility — removes the structural ceiling that conventional loans impose.

The cost of that flexibility: higher rates, mandatory down payments of 20–25%, prepayment penalties, and stricter requirements in low-yield markets.

If your property cashflows at a DSCR of 1.15 or better, you have a 5+ year hold plan, and you're either self-employed, approaching your conventional loan limit, or buying in an LLC — DSCR is likely your best financing path.
