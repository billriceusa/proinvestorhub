# HELOC on a Rental Property: The Real Estate Investor's Complete Guide (2026)

Yes, you can get a HELOC on a rental property — but it works differently than borrowing against your primary home, and far fewer lenders offer them. A rental property HELOC lets you tap the equity in an income-producing property as a revolving credit line, without touching your existing mortgage rate or selling the asset. Requirements are stricter: minimum 700 credit score, no more than 75–80% combined loan-to-value, and typically six months of cash reserves. But for investors who have built meaningful equity in a performing rental, it's often the most capital-efficient tool available for funding renovations, bridging acquisitions, or seeding the next deal.

This guide covers how to qualify, the actual math on cost versus return, when to choose a HELOC over a cash-out refinance, and where to find the lenders who actually do these loans.

---

## How a Rental Property HELOC Works

A HELOC is a revolving line of credit secured by real property. During the **draw period** — typically five to ten years — you can borrow up to your approved limit, repay, and borrow again, paying interest only on the outstanding balance. After the draw period closes, the line converts to a **repayment period** (usually ten to twenty years) during which you pay down principal plus interest and can no longer draw new funds.

On a primary residence, this is a fairly standard product. On a rental property, the mechanics are identical, but lenders treat it very differently:

- **Fewer lenders participate.** Most retail banks and large mortgage shops don't offer HELOCs on non-owner-occupied properties. You're looking at portfolio lenders, regional banks, credit unions, and a small handful of national lenders with dedicated investment property programs.
- **Rates run higher.** Expect a premium of 0.5–1.5% above the equivalent primary residence HELOC. Lenders price in the additional risk of a property you don't live in — the theory being you'll walk away from a rental before you'll walk away from your home.
- **Underwriting scrutinizes the property, not just you.** Your personal financials matter, but the lender will also evaluate the rental's income, occupancy history, and income stability.

## The 5 Numbers That Determine Whether You Qualify

Every lender has slightly different thresholds, but these five metrics govern almost every rental property HELOC application:

| Metric | Investment Property | Primary Residence |
|---|---|---|
| Minimum credit score | 700–720 | 650–680 |
| Max combined LTV | 75–80% | 85–90% |
| Max debt-to-income (DTI) | Up to 50% (with rental income counted) | 43% |
| Cash reserves required | 6 months of payments | Often none |
| Minimum equity stake | 20% | 15% |

**Credit score (700 minimum):** Most lenders require 700–720. Some portfolio lenders will go to 680 for strong borrowers with significant reserves, but expect a rate penalty. Below 700, your options narrow sharply.

**Combined LTV of 75–80%:** Your total debt against the property — existing mortgage balance plus the HELOC limit — can't exceed roughly 75–80% of the appraised value. This is meaningfully tighter than the 85–90% available on primary homes. A property worth $300,000 with a $170,000 mortgage gives you roughly $55,000–$70,000 of HELOC capacity at those limits.

**DTI up to 50%:** Unlike primary home HELOCs, lenders for investment properties often count documented rental income toward your qualifying income, which can substantially reduce your effective DTI. Get your Schedule E or current leases ready.

**Six months of reserves:** This is the requirement that catches investors off guard most often. Lenders want to see that you can cover six months of full mortgage payments — on the property being pledged and sometimes on your primary residence — in liquid assets. For investors with multiple properties, this can be a meaningful capital requirement.

**Minimum 20% equity:** You need to own at least 20% of the property outright. Combined with the LTV cap, this means you can't tap all your equity — there will always be a floor the lender requires you to leave in.

## The 4 Ways Investors Actually Use Rental Property HELOCs

The Bankrates and Chase pages of the world will tell you to "use it for renovations or to buy another property." Here's what that actually looks like in investor practice:

### 1. Fund the next acquisition (without selling the first)

This is the most powerful use. Rather than waiting to save a new down payment or doing a full cash-out refinance, you draw from your rental property HELOC for the down payment and closing costs on a second property. Your existing mortgage terms stay intact, you access capital in days rather than months, and the draw only costs you interest on what you actually use.

### 2. BRRRR rehab financing

The [BRRRR strategy](/calculators/brrrr) depends on buying below market value, renovating, and then refinancing at the higher post-renovation value to recycle capital. A rental property HELOC is a natural fit for the rehab phase: draw funds as needed during construction, pay interest only, then use the long-term refinance proceeds to repay the line. This preserves your cash reserves and keeps short-term carry costs low. Run the numbers with our [BRRRR calculator](/calculators/brrrr) before deciding whether HELOC or hard money is the better vehicle for your specific rehab.

### 3. Force appreciation through strategic renovations

Kitchen and bath upgrades, additional units, separate meters, or converting to short-term rental setup can significantly increase both property value and rental income. A HELOC lets you stage these investments — drawing capital when the project is underway and the return is clearer — rather than committing a lump sum up front. The interest you pay on rental-property HELOC funds used for property improvements is generally deductible as a business expense on Schedule E [VERIFY: confirm Schedule E treatment with your CPA].

### 4. Bridge a closing gap

Real estate contracts don't always align perfectly. If you're selling one property and closing on another within days of each other, a rental property HELOC can bridge the gap — giving you the funds to close on the purchase before the sale proceeds arrive. This is cheaper than a formal bridge loan in most cases, assuming you already have the line in place.

## The Real Math: A Worked Example

Let's make this concrete. Say you own a rental property worth $350,000 with a remaining mortgage balance of $175,000.

**Your equity:** $350,000 − $175,000 = $175,000  
**Maximum CLTV at 80%:** $350,000 × 0.80 = $280,000  
**Maximum HELOC limit:** $280,000 − $175,000 = **$105,000**

At a rate of prime + 1.5% — call it 9.0% on today's prime rate [VERIFY: current Fed prime rate] — full utilization of the $105,000 line costs roughly **$788/month in interest**. If you only draw $60,000, interest drops to approximately **$450/month**.

Now compare that to what the capital produces:
- $60,000 HELOC draw funds a **$240,000 acquisition** (25% down plus closing costs)
- That property generates $1,800/month in gross rent
- After PITI on the new mortgage (~$1,350/month at current rates [VERIFY: current investment property mortgage rates]) and HELOC carry cost ($450/month), net cash position is -$0/month initially — but you've acquired an asset with $60,000+ in equity and long-term appreciation upside.

Over three years, you repay the HELOC draw from rental income on the new property, restoring your credit line for the next deal. Use our [rental cash flow calculator](/calculators/rental-cashflow) to model this against your specific numbers before committing.

## HELOC vs. Cash-Out Refinance: Which Is Right for Investors?

Both tools let you access equity in an investment property. The right choice depends on three variables:

| Decision Factor | HELOC Wins | Cash-Out Refi Wins |
|---|---|---|
| **You need flexibility** | Multiple draws over time, only pay interest on what you use | Lump sum up front |
| **Your current rate is below market** | Keep your existing mortgage intact | Your rate is at or above market anyway |
| **Amount needed** | Smaller, variable amounts | Large, known lump sum |
| **Rate preference** | OK with variable exposure | Need a fixed rate |
| **Time horizon** | Shorter-term need you'll repay within the draw period | Long-term permanent capital access |

One important note: **a HELOC doesn't reset your mortgage clock.** A cash-out refinance replaces your existing loan with a new one, resetting amortization and potentially raising your rate if you're refinancing out of a sub-5% mortgage. If you locked a 3.5% rate in 2021, keeping it intact while accessing a HELOC is almost always the right call at current market rates.

## The 4 Gotchas That Trip Investors Up

### 1. Most big banks quietly don't offer them

Wells Fargo paused its home equity lending program [VERIFY: current status of Wells Fargo HELOC offerings]. Chase offers HELOCs on investment properties but with limited geographic availability and stricter internal guidelines than their marketing suggests. The practical reality: retail banking isn't the right channel. You'll have better results with portfolio lenders, community banks, and credit unions that hold their own loans and make exceptions based on the full borrower relationship.

### 2. The appraisal goes deeper than you expect

Investment property HELOC appraisals routinely include a **Rent Schedule (Form 1007)**, which documents current market rent. Some lenders require two appraisals to validate value. Budget $600–$1,200 for the appraisal process [VERIFY: current appraisal fee range in your market] and allow three to six weeks in your timeline.

### 3. Variable rate risk is real

HELOCs are almost universally variable rate, tied to the prime rate. If the Fed raises rates 200 basis points, your carry cost on a $100,000 draw goes up by $2,000/year overnight. For short-term draws this is manageable. For long-term portfolio financing, variable exposure can erode returns significantly. Some lenders allow you to lock a portion of the balance at a fixed rate — ask specifically about this feature when shopping.

### 4. Annual fees and early termination clauses

Many investment property HELOCs come with annual fees ($50–$200/year) and early termination penalties if you close the line within two to three years. If you draw the funds, repay, and then close the line quickly, you may owe the penalty anyway. Read the fee schedule in full before signing.

## Tax Deductibility on Rental Properties

This is where rental property HELOCs differ meaningfully from primary home HELOCs. Interest on a HELOC secured by a rental property and used for **rental business purposes** (repairs, renovations, acquiring additional rental property, operating expenses) is generally deductible as a business expense on **Schedule E** — not subject to the $750,000 qualified residence interest cap that governs primary home mortgage deductions [VERIFY: consult your CPA; tax treatment depends on specific use of funds and your individual tax situation].

The practical implication: if you draw $80,000 to fund renovations on your rental and pay $7,200 in interest over the year, that $7,200 is a rental business expense that reduces your taxable rental income. This materially improves the after-tax cost of the capital.

Document the use of HELOC proceeds carefully. Keep a dedicated account for the drawn funds and trace every dollar to rental-related expenses. Commingling HELOC proceeds with personal funds creates audit risk and may compromise the deductibility.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Can I get a HELOC on a rental property I just bought?**  
Most lenders require seasoning of six to twelve months before they'll extend a HELOC on an investment property. You need to have owned the property and established its rental income history before most underwriters will approve the application.

**Does my rental property HELOC affect my ability to buy more properties?**  
The HELOC draws against your credit report as revolving debt. High utilization increases your DTI and can affect qualifying for new mortgages. If you're planning to use HELOC proceeds to acquire more properties, keep the draw amount in line with what the new property's income can support in your DTI calculation.

**What's the minimum equity I need?**  
Most lenders require at least 20% equity remaining after the HELOC is in place. At an 80% combined LTV, a $300,000 property with a $200,000 balance leaves you $40,000 in potential line capacity. At a more conservative 75% CLTV, that same property yields only $25,000.

**Can I use the HELOC from one rental to buy a different type of property?**  
Yes — the proceeds can generally be used for any purpose, including acquiring commercial property, raw land, or a vacation rental. The deductibility of the interest changes depending on the use of funds. Your tax deductibility depends on what you use the money for, not what you secured it against [VERIFY: consult CPA].
